## What Are Kanji?

**Kanji** (漢字) are logographic characters originally borrowed from Chinese. Each kanji has a meaning and one or more readings. Japanese uses kanji alongside hiragana and katakana in the same sentences.

There are about **2,136 common-use kanji** (常用漢字), but JLPT N5 only requires you to know approximately **80–100 kanji**. This is a very manageable number.

## Two Readings: On and Kun

Most kanji have two types of readings:

- **On-reading (音読み, onyomi)**: The Chinese-derived reading, used in compounds (jukugo)
- **Kun-reading (訓読み, kunyomi)**: The native Japanese reading, used alone or with okurigana

> Example: **山**
> - Kun: やま (yama) — "mountain" standing alone
> - On: さん (san) — used in compounds like 富士山 (ふじさん, Mt. Fuji)

In practice: **kun-reading** when the kanji is alone; **on-reading** when it's in a multi-kanji compound. (There are exceptions, but this rule holds most of the time.)

## How to Learn Kanji Efficiently

1. **Learn radicals** — most kanji are built from simpler components called radicals (部首, bushu). Recognizing radicals helps you guess meanings.

2. **Learn kanji in words** — it's easier to remember 日本語 (nihongo, Japanese language) than to memorize 日, 本, 語 separately.

3. **Write them** — tracing strokes builds muscle memory. Start with low-stroke kanji.

4. **Use SRS (Spaced Repetition)** — apps like Anki help you review kanji at optimal intervals.

5. **Read a lot** — the more you encounter kanji in context, the faster they become automatic.

## Stroke Order

Stroke order follows consistent rules. The general principle is:
- Top before bottom
- Left before right
- Horizontal before vertical (usually)
- Outside before inside for enclosures

Correct stroke order makes writing faster and more consistent, and is required for handwriting recognition.
