## The Basic Numbers

Japanese has two sets of numbers — **Sino-Japanese** (from Chinese) and **Native Japanese**. For most everyday counting, Sino-Japanese is used.

### Sino-Japanese (1–10)

| Number | Kanji | Hiragana | Romaji |
|--------|-------|----------|--------|
| 1 | 一 | いち | ichi |
| 2 | 二 | に | ni |
| 3 | 三 | さん | san |
| 4 | 四 | し / よん | shi / yon |
| 5 | 五 | ご | go |
| 6 | 六 | ろく | roku |
| 7 | 七 | しち / なな | shichi / nana |
| 8 | 八 | はち | hachi |
| 9 | 九 | く / きゅう | ku / kyuu |
| 10 | 十 | じゅう | juu |

> **4 and 9 have alternate readings:** 4 = よん (yon) is preferred in many contexts because し sounds like 死 (death). Similarly, 9 = きゅう is preferred over く which sounds like 苦 (suffering). You'll encounter both in real Japanese.

## Building Larger Numbers

Japanese uses a simple additive system:

- 11 = 十一 (じゅういち) — ten-one
- 20 = 二十 (にじゅう) — two-ten
- 35 = 三十五 (さんじゅうご) — three-ten-five
- 100 = 百 (ひゃく)
- 200 = 二百 (にひゃく)
- 1,000 = 千 (せん)
- 10,000 = 万 (まん)

### Key Units

| Value | Kanji | Reading |
|-------|-------|---------|
| 100 | 百 | ひゃく (hyaku) |
| 1,000 | 千 | せん (sen) |
| 10,000 | 万 | まん (man) |

**Important:** Japanese counts in units of 10,000, not 1,000. So:

- 50,000 = 五万 (ごまん) — five ten-thousands
- 100,000 = 十万 (じゅうまん) — ten ten-thousands
- 1,000,000 = 百万 (ひゃくまん) — hundred ten-thousands

### Number Pronunciation Changes

Some numbers change pronunciation due to phonetic collisions:

| Number | Standard | Actual |
|--------|---------|--------|
| 300 | さんひゃく | **さんびゃく** |
| 600 | ろくひゃく | **ろっぴゃく** |
| 800 | はちひゃく | **はっぴゃく** |
| 3,000 | さんせん | **さんぜん** |
| 8,000 | はちせん | **はっせん** |

## Native Japanese Numbers (1–10)

Used for counting general objects, and in some traditional contexts:

| Number | Reading | Kanji |
|--------|---------|-------|
| 1 | ひとつ (hitotsu) | 一つ |
| 2 | ふたつ (futatsu) | 二つ |
| 3 | みっつ (mittsu) | 三つ |
| 4 | よっつ (yottsu) | 四つ |
| 5 | いつつ (itsutsu) | 五つ |
| 6 | むっつ (muttsu) | 六つ |
| 7 | ななつ (nanatsu) | 七つ |
| 8 | やっつ (yattsu) | 八つ |
| 9 | ここのつ (kokonotsu) | 九つ |
| 10 | とお (too) | 十 |

After 10, native Japanese numbers are rarely used — use Sino-Japanese instead.

## Counters (助数詞)

Japanese uses **counters** — suffixes that attach to numbers based on what is being counted. This is one of the most important grammar features for N5.

### Essential N5 Counters

| Counter | Reading | Used for | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| 〜個 | こ (ko) | small objects | りんご三個 (3 apples) |
| 〜本 | ほん (hon) | long thin objects | えんぴつ二本 (2 pencils) |
| 〜枚 | まい (mai) | flat thin objects | かみ五枚 (5 sheets of paper) |
| 〜冊 | さつ (satsu) | bound books | ほん三冊 (3 books) |
| 〜台 | だい (dai) | machines/vehicles | くるま一台 (1 car) |
| 〜匹 | ひき (hiki) | small animals | ねこ二匹 (2 cats) |
| 〜頭 | とう (tou) | large animals | いぬ三頭 (3 dogs) |
| 〜人 | にん (nin) | people | さんにん (3 people) |
| 〜杯 | はい (hai) | cups/bowls | みずいっぱい (1 cup of water) |
| 〜階 | かい (kai) | floors of a building | さんがい (3rd floor) |

> **Special:** 1 person = ひとり (一人), 2 people = ふたり (二人) — these are exceptions using native Japanese numbers.

## Telling Time

Time uses the counter 時 (じ) for hours and 分 (ふん/ぷん) for minutes:

- 一時 (いちじ) — 1 o'clock
- 三時半 (さんじはん) — 3:30 (半 = half)
- 何時ですか？ (なんじですか？) — What time is it?
- 七時十五分 (しちじじゅうごふん) — 7:15

## Dates

- 〜月 (がつ) for months: 一月 (January), 二月 (February)...
- 〜日 (にち/か) for days of month: 一日 (ついたち, 1st), 二日 (ふつか, 2nd)...

The first 10 days of the month have special native Japanese readings — these are frequently tested on JLPT N5.
