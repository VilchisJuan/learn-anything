## What is Hiragana?

**Hiragana** (平仮名) is the first writing system you must learn in Japanese. It is a syllabic alphabet — each character represents a sound (a syllable), not a meaning. There are 46 basic characters, plus voiced and combination sounds.

Every Japanese sentence uses hiragana. It is used for:

- Native Japanese words
- Grammatical endings (particles, verb conjugations)
- Words that have no kanji or whose kanji is too rare to use
- Children's books and furigana (pronunciation guides over kanji)

> Think of hiragana as the "handwriting" of Japanese — round, flowing, and essential.

## The 46 Basic Characters

Hiragana is organized into a **10×5 grid** called the *gojūon* (fifty sounds). The rows represent consonant sounds, the columns represent vowel sounds (a, i, u, e, o).

## How to Read the Chart

The chart follows this structure:

| Consonant | -a | -i | -u | -e | -o |
|-----------|----|----|----|----|-----|
| ∅ (vowels)| あ a | い i | う u | え e | お o |
| k- | か ka | き ki | く ku | け ke | こ ko |
| s- | さ sa | し **shi** | す su | せ se | そ so |
| t- | た ta | ち **chi** | つ **tsu** | て te | と to |
| n- | な na | に ni | ぬ nu | ね ne | の no |
| h- | は ha | ひ hi | ふ **fu** | へ he | ほ ho |

Notice the irregular pronunciations: **し = shi** (not si), **ち = chi** (not ti), **つ = tsu** (not tu), **ふ = fu** (not hu).

## Voiced Sounds (濁音)

By adding **dakuten** (゛) — two small dashes — to か, さ, た, and は rows, you get voiced variants:

- **か → が** (ka → ga): が行 — ga, gi, gu, ge, go
- **さ → ざ** (sa → za): ざ行 — za, ji, zu, ze, zo
- **た → だ** (ta → da): だ行 — da, ji, zu, de, do
- **は → ば** (ha → ba): ば行 — ba, bi, bu, be, bo

The **は** row also gets a semi-voiced variant with **handakuten** (゜) — a small circle:

- **は → ぱ** (ha → pa): ぱ行 — pa, pi, pu, pe, po

## Combination Sounds (拗音)

Combining い-column characters with small や, ゆ, よ creates new sounds:

- **き + や → きゃ** (kya)
- **し + ゆ → しゅ** (shu)
- **ち + よ → ちょ** (cho)
- **に + や → にゃ** (nya)
- **ひ + よ → ひょ** (hyo)

The small version of や, ゆ, よ (ゃ, ゅ, ょ) signals that it combines with the preceding character.

## Special Characters

**っ (small tsu)** — doubles the following consonant:
- きって (kitte) — stamp
- ざっし (zasshi) — magazine

**ん (n)** — the only standalone consonant. It never starts a word:
- ほん (hon) — book
- にほん (nihon) — Japan

## Learning Strategy

The fastest way to learn hiragana:

1. **Learn by rows** — master all 5 あ-row vowels first, then か-row, then さ-row...
2. **Write them** — writing each character 10× is more effective than just reading
3. **Read real words** — as soon as you know 2–3 rows, start reading simple Japanese words
4. **Use mnemonics** — many learners find visual associations helpful (e.g., あ looks like an "a" rotated)

After 1–2 weeks of daily practice, most learners can read basic hiragana fluently.
