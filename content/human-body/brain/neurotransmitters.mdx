## Chemical Messengers of the Brain

When an action potential reaches a synapse, it triggers the release of chemical molecules into the synaptic cleft. These molecules — **neurotransmitters** — bind to receptors on the postsynaptic cell and alter its activity. The brain uses dozens of distinct neurotransmitters, each with characteristic anatomical distributions, receptors, and physiological roles. Understanding neurotransmitter systems is essential for understanding both normal brain function and the pharmacology of psychiatric and neurological drugs.

## Glutamate: The Brain's Main Excitatory Signal

**Glutamate** is the most abundant excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain, used at the majority of fast excitatory synapses throughout the cortex, hippocampus, cerebellum, and spinal cord. It acts primarily on three families of ionotropic receptors: **AMPA**, **NMDA**, and **kainate** receptors — named for the synthetic compounds that selectively activate them.

AMPA receptors mediate the fast component of excitatory transmission. NMDA receptors are slower and, crucially, are blocked by Mg²⁺ at resting membrane potentials; they require both glutamate binding *and* postsynaptic depolarization to open. This coincidence-detection property makes NMDA receptors the molecular gate for [synaptic plasticity](/human-body/brain/plasticity) and memory formation. Excessive glutamate release — as occurs during a stroke when ischemic neurons dump their contents — triggers **excitotoxicity**: NMDA-mediated Ca²⁺ overload that kills neurons. This makes glutamate receptors an important target in neuroprotective research.

## GABA: The Brain's Main Inhibitory Signal

**GABA** (gamma-aminobutyric acid) is the principal inhibitory neurotransmitter, balancing glutamate's excitation throughout the brain. It acts on **GABA-A receptors** (ionotropic Cl⁻ channels) and **GABA-B receptors** (metabotropic K⁺ regulators). GABA-A receptor activation hyperpolarizes neurons and reduces their probability of firing. Given that the brain's excitatory and inhibitory systems must remain precisely balanced to prevent seizures, GABA's role is literally stabilizing.

Many commonly used drugs target GABA-A receptors. **Benzodiazepines** (diazepam, lorazepam) enhance GABA-A currents by increasing the frequency of channel opening, producing anxiolytic and sedative effects. **Barbiturates** (phenobarbital) increase the duration of channel opening. **Alcohol** also modulates GABA-A receptors, contributing to its sedating and disinhibiting effects.

## Dopamine: Reward, Movement, and More

**Dopamine** is a catecholamine neurotransmitter produced primarily in two midbrain regions: the **substantia nigra pars compacta** (projecting to the striatum via the nigrostriatal pathway) and the **ventral tegmental area** (projecting to the nucleus accumbens and prefrontal cortex via the mesolimbic and mesocortical pathways).

Dopamine acts not as a simple reward signal but as a **reward prediction error** signal — it encodes the difference between expected and received reward. When a reward is better than expected, dopamine neurons fire; when it is worse than expected, they are suppressed; when exactly as expected, they show no change. This teaching signal is fundamental to reinforcement learning and habit formation.

The striatal dopamine system also controls initiation and scaling of movement; its loss underlies the motor deficits of Parkinson's disease. The cortical dopamine system supports working memory and executive control. Dopamine dysregulation has been implicated in schizophrenia (hyperactivity in mesolimbic circuits), addiction, and ADHD.

## Serotonin: Mood, Sleep, and the Gut

**Serotonin** (5-hydroxytryptamine, 5-HT) is synthesized primarily in the **raphe nuclei** of the brainstem, which project widely to the cortex, hippocampus, basal ganglia, and spinal cord. A commonly cited fact is that roughly 90–95% of the body's serotonin is actually located in the gastrointestinal tract, where it regulates gut motility — a reminder that neurotransmitters have functions far beyond the brain.

Serotonin influences mood, emotional processing, [sleep](/human-body/brain/sleep), appetite, and social behavior. The **monoamine hypothesis of depression** proposes (in its simplest form) that depression reflects insufficient serotonin (and/or dopamine/norepinephrine) signaling — a model supported by the clinical efficacy of SSRIs, though the full picture is considerably more complex. Serotonin acts on at least 14 distinct receptor subtypes, most of which are metabotropic; the exception is 5-HT₃, an ionotropic receptor targeted by antiemetics.

## Acetylcholine: Attention, Memory, and the Neuromuscular Junction

**Acetylcholine (ACh)** was the first neurotransmitter discovered, identified by Otto Loewi in 1921. In the brain, ACh is produced by neurons in the **basal forebrain** (including the nucleus basalis of Meynert) that project throughout the cortex and hippocampus, modulating attention, arousal, and [memory](/human-body/brain/memory). The significant degeneration of basal forebrain cholinergic neurons in Alzheimer's disease — and the resulting loss of cortical ACh — underlies many of its cognitive symptoms. This is the basis for **acetylcholinesterase inhibitors** (donepezil, rivastigmine) as a treatment approach.

In the peripheral nervous system, ACh is the neurotransmitter at the **neuromuscular junction**, triggering muscle contraction. It is also the transmitter of all preganglionic autonomic fibers and postganglionic parasympathetic fibers. ACh acts on two receptor families: **nicotinic receptors** (ionotropic, named for the agonist nicotine) and **muscarinic receptors** (metabotropic, named for the mushroom toxin muscarine).

## Norepinephrine: Alertness and Stress

**Norepinephrine** (noradrenaline) is synthesized primarily in the **locus coeruleus** of the brainstem, which sends diffuse projections throughout the cortex, hippocampus, and spinal cord. It modulates arousal, attention, vigilance, and the brain's response to novelty and stress. Locus coeruleus neurons fire in bursts during unexpected or salient events, broadcasting a "pay attention" signal to large portions of the forebrain.

Norepinephrine also plays a peripheral role as part of the sympathetic nervous system's fight-or-flight response. Many antidepressants (SNRIs, TCAs) target norepinephrine reuptake, and the alpha and beta adrenergic receptors it acts on are targets for drugs ranging from blood pressure medications to treatments for PTSD.

## Endorphins and Neuropeptides

**Endorphins** are endogenous opioid peptides released in response to pain and vigorous exercise. They act on opioid receptors to produce analgesia and euphoria — the same receptors targeted by morphine and heroin. **Substance P** signals pain information in the spinal cord. **Neuropeptide Y** regulates appetite. These neuropeptide transmitters are typically co-released with classical neurotransmitters and fine-tune circuit function through slower, longer-lasting metabotropic actions.

## Ionotropic vs. Metabotropic Receptors

Neurotransmitter receptors fall into two broad structural categories. **Ionotropic receptors** are ligand-gated ion channels: binding opens the channel directly, producing fast (millisecond) changes in membrane potential. **Metabotropic receptors** (G-protein-coupled receptors) trigger intracellular signaling cascades that can open or close distant ion channels, regulate gene expression, and remodel synaptic structure over timescales from seconds to hours. Most neurotransmitters can act on both types of receptor, enabling both fast signaling and slow modulation in the same synapse.
