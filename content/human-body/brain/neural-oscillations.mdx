## Rhythms in the Brain

Individual neurons fire in irregular bursts. Yet when neurons are recorded collectively — as populations — they often show striking rhythmicity. This collective oscillatory activity can be detected by **electroencephalography (EEG)**, which records voltage fluctuations at the scalp, or by intracranial electrodes placed directly on or within brain tissue. The discovery that the brain oscillates across multiple frequencies, in patterns that vary with mental state, task, and disease, has opened a rich area of research at the intersection of physics, neuroscience, and clinical medicine.

The EEG was first applied to humans by Hans Berger in 1924. He noticed that the resting brain produces regular oscillations in the 8–13 Hz range — which he called **alpha waves** — that are suppressed when the subject opens their eyes or performs a task. Berger's discovery inaugurated the systematic study of brain rhythms.

## The Five Major Frequency Bands

Neural oscillations are conventionally organized into five frequency bands, each with characteristic functional correlates.

### Delta (0.5–4 Hz)

**Delta** is the slowest frequency band and is most prominent during deep **slow-wave sleep (N3 stage)**. Large-amplitude, synchronous delta waves reflect a state in which the thalamus and cortex oscillate in a bistable pattern — alternating between "up states" (brief bursts of cortical activity) and "down states" (near silence). This oscillation is thought to be important for [memory consolidation during sleep](/human-body/brain/sleep), coordinating hippocampal replay events with cortical receiving sites. In wakefulness, excess delta activity typically indicates brain injury, tumor, or profound metabolic disturbance.

### Theta (4–8 Hz)

**Theta** oscillations are most prominently recorded in the hippocampus, where they are strongly associated with active exploration, spatial navigation, and memory encoding. In the hippocampus, theta arises when an animal is moving through space and is abolished during immobility. Theta oscillations serve as a **temporal scaffold** for the precise timing of hippocampal place cell firing: different neurons fire at different phases of the theta cycle, encoding the animal's position with millisecond precision in a code called **phase precession** (discovered by John O'Keefe and Michael Recce, 1993).

In humans, frontal and temporal theta increases during working memory tasks, suggesting theta plays a role in maintaining and manipulating information actively held in mind.

### Alpha (8–13 Hz)

**Alpha** oscillations, Berger's original discovery, are the dominant EEG rhythm during relaxed wakefulness with eyes closed. They are generated primarily in occipital and parietal cortices and are powerfully suppressed (desynchronized) when the eyes open or attention is engaged — a phenomenon called **alpha blocking** or **event-related desynchronization (ERD)**.

Alpha is no longer viewed merely as an "idle" rhythm. Contemporary evidence suggests alpha reflects **active inhibition**: cortical areas that are not currently needed for a task show enhanced alpha, effectively reducing their excitability and preventing them from interfering with task-relevant processing. Alpha amplitude in task-irrelevant areas predicts how well sensory stimuli in task-relevant areas are processed — an elegant gating mechanism.

### Beta (13–30 Hz)

**Beta** oscillations are prominent over motor cortex during movement preparation and maintenance of stable motor states. Paradoxically, beta is suppressed just before and during actual movement and rebounds afterward — a pattern that has been interpreted as reflecting "maintenance of the current motor state." Beta is also elevated in Parkinson's disease in the basal ganglia-cortical loops, where abnormally synchronous beta oscillations are thought to impede movement initiation — the very core symptom of the disease. Deep brain stimulation, a treatment for Parkinson's, reduces pathological beta in the subthalamic nucleus and improves motor control.

### Gamma (30–100 Hz)

**Gamma** oscillations are the fastest and most closely correlated with active, attentive, and perceptually rich processing. Gamma is enhanced in cortical areas processing a currently attended stimulus, across sensory modalities. **Gamma-band synchronization** — two brain regions oscillating in gamma at the same phase — has been proposed as a mechanism of **feature binding**: the linking of simultaneously processed features (color, shape, motion) into a unified perceptual object.

Gamma oscillations are generated primarily by local circuits of fast-spiking inhibitory interneurons (parvalbumin-positive basket cells and chandelier cells) entraining pyramidal neuron populations. Dysfunction of these inhibitory interneurons — as observed in post-mortem studies of schizophrenia — is thought to impair gamma generation and contribute to the perceptual and cognitive symptoms of the disorder.

## Cross-Frequency Coupling

Oscillations at different frequencies interact. The most studied form is **theta-gamma coupling** (or phase-amplitude coupling): the phase of a slow theta oscillation modulates the amplitude of fast gamma oscillations nested within it. In the hippocampus during memory encoding, each theta cycle contains several gamma bursts, with different neuron assemblies firing at different gamma cycles within the theta cycle. This hierarchical temporal organization is thought to provide a multiplexing mechanism — packaging information from multiple items into a single working memory load, with each gamma cycle holding one item.

## Neural Avalanches and Criticality

At a network level, the brain appears to operate near a **critical point** between ordered and chaotic dynamics. Evidence for this comes from the observation of **neural avalanches** — cascades of neural activity that follow a power law in size and duration, a hallmark of criticality in physical systems. Operating near criticality maximizes the brain's dynamic range (sensitivity to inputs), information storage, and information transmission. Pathological states — seizure (hypersynchrony) and deep anesthesia (hyposynchrony) — may represent departures from criticality in opposite directions.
