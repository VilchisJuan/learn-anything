## Beyond Neurons

For most of the twentieth century, glial cells were treated as the brain's background — inert scaffolding that held neurons in place while the "real" work happened in synaptic circuits. The Latin root of the word reflects this dismissive history: "glia" means glue. That view has been overturned decisively. Glial cells are active participants in virtually every aspect of brain function, from regulating the chemical environment of synapses to shaping the brain's immune response to injury and disease.

The brain contains roughly equal numbers of neurons and glial cells — approximately 85 billion of each in the human brain. (An older claim that glia outnumber neurons 10:1 was based on flawed estimates and has been corrected by systematic cell-counting studies, most notably by Azevedo and colleagues in 2009.) Each glial cell type has a distinct morphology, molecular identity, and function.

## Astrocytes

**Astrocytes** are the most abundant glial cells in the brain. Their star-shaped morphology (from the Greek *astron*, "star") allows them to extend fine processes that contact both synapses and blood vessels simultaneously. This strategic positioning gives them a dual role: they regulate the chemical environment around synapses, and they form a critical component of the **blood-brain barrier**.

At synapses, astrocytes take up neurotransmitters — particularly glutamate — from the synaptic cleft after a neuron fires, preventing excitotoxic buildup. They recycle these neurotransmitters and return them to the presynaptic neuron in a process essential for sustained neural communication. Astrocytes can also release their own signaling molecules, called **gliotransmitters**, and communicate with one another through calcium waves that spread across large tissue areas — a form of long-range signaling that does not involve electrical action potentials.

Astrocytic endfeet wrap tightly around the capillaries supplying the brain, contributing the cellular machinery that enforces the blood-brain barrier's selective permeability. They also help match local blood flow to local neural activity — the phenomenon underlying the BOLD signal in fMRI brain imaging.

## Oligodendrocytes

**Oligodendrocytes** are the myelin-producing cells of the central nervous system. A single oligodendrocyte extends flat processes that wrap around multiple axon segments in a tight spiral, forming the **myelin sheath** — a fatty insulating layer that dramatically accelerates signal transmission. Without myelin, action potentials travel slowly and unreliably; with it, conduction velocities can reach 120 meters per second.

Oligodendrocytes are also metabolically supportive of the axons they wrap, providing lactate and other metabolic substrates directly through gap junctions. This trophic function turns out to be vital: mice engineered to lack myelin proteins but retain the myelin sheath still suffer axonal degeneration, indicating that the metabolic support function is distinct from the insulating function.

**Multiple sclerosis (MS)** is the most prominent disease involving oligodendrocytes. In MS, the immune system attacks myelin sheaths in the CNS, producing plaques of demyelination that disrupt signal conduction. Symptoms vary depending on which white matter tracts are affected and can include vision loss, weakness, and cognitive difficulties.

## Microglia

**Microglia** are the brain's resident immune cells. Constituting roughly 10–15% of all brain cells, they are derived from a different lineage than other glia — they originate in the yolk sac during embryonic development and migrate into the brain early in gestation, before the blood-brain barrier closes. They are thus distinct from the monocytes and macrophages that circulate in the bloodstream.

In the healthy brain, microglia are not dormant. They extend and retract their processes continuously, surveying the local environment. When they detect signs of injury, infection, or abnormal protein aggregates, they change shape, migrate to the affected area, and mount an inflammatory response — phagocytosing (engulfing) debris, dead cells, and pathogens. Critically, microglia also participate in **synaptic pruning** during brain development, eliminating weaker or redundant synaptic connections in a process guided by complement proteins. Dysregulation of this pruning process has been implicated in schizophrenia and autism spectrum disorder.

In chronic neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer's, microglia can become chronically activated in a state that amplifies neuroinflammation rather than resolving it.

## Schwann Cells

**Schwann cells** perform in the peripheral nervous system the same myelinating function that oligodendrocytes perform in the CNS. One key difference: a single Schwann cell myelinates one segment of one axon, whereas a single oligodendrocyte can myelinate segments on many different axons. This architectural difference is thought to explain why the PNS recovers more readily from injury than the CNS — Schwann cells can remyelinate regenerating axons, while CNS remyelination by oligodendrocyte precursors is far less efficient.

## Ependymal Cells

**Ependymal cells** line the brain's four fluid-filled **ventricles** and the central canal of the spinal cord. They are arranged in a single layer and bear cilia on their apical surface that beat rhythmically to circulate **cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)**. Specialized ependymal cells in the choroid plexus secrete CSF — roughly 500 milliliters per day — which cushions the brain, removes metabolic waste, and maintains ionic homeostasis. CSF is continuously produced and reabsorbed; at any one moment, the brain and spinal cord are bathed in only about 150 mL.

## Glia and Disease

Glial dysfunction underlies a wide range of neurological conditions. **Gliomas** — tumors arising from glial cells (most commonly astrocytes and oligodendrocyte precursors) — are the most common primary brain tumors in adults, and glioblastoma multiforme is among the most lethal cancers known. Reactive gliosis, the scarring response of astrocytes to injury, can be both protective and harmful: it limits the spread of damage but also impedes axon regeneration.

Understanding glia is no longer optional for understanding the brain. It is central to it.
