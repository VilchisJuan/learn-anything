## Beyond the Cortex

The cerebral cortex, with its elaborate folded surface and layered architecture, is the most visible and distinctly human part of the brain. But deep beneath it lie structures that are evolutionarily older and in many ways more immediately critical for survival. These **subcortical structures** regulate the flow of sensory information, consolidate memories, process emotions, control hormone release, and coordinate movement. No account of the brain is complete without them.

## The Hippocampus

The **hippocampus** (Greek for "seahorse," which its curved shape resembles) lies deep within the medial temporal lobe, and is the brain region most associated with **memory consolidation**. Patient H.M. (Henry Molaison), who had both hippocampi surgically removed in 1953 to treat intractable epilepsy, lost the ability to form new explicit memories entirely — he could not remember meeting a person five minutes after being introduced, yet retained memories from before the surgery and could still learn new motor skills. His case established beyond doubt that the hippocampus is essential for converting short-term memories into long-lasting declarative memories, a process described more fully in [Memory](/human-body/brain/memory).

The hippocampus also plays a central role in **spatial navigation**. In the 1970s, John O'Keefe discovered **place cells** — hippocampal neurons that fire selectively when an animal is in a specific location in an environment. This spatial map function, and the closely related discovery of grid cells in the adjacent entorhinal cortex by Edvard and May-Britt Moser, earned the 2014 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. The hippocampus appears to use the same neural machinery for representing space and for organizing memories in sequence — a deep connection between navigation and narrative memory.

## The Amygdala

Sitting just anterior to the hippocampus, the **amygdala** (Greek for "almond") is a cluster of nuclei most famous for its role in **fear and emotional memory**. The basolateral amygdala receives sensory input from virtually every modality and evaluates stimuli for emotional salience, particularly threats. When a conditioned threat is detected, the amygdala activates the **central nucleus**, which outputs to the hypothalamus (triggering stress hormone release), the brainstem (triggering freezing behavior), and the cortex (triggering attention and fear awareness).

Patient SM, a woman with bilateral amygdala damage caused by Urbach-Wiethe disease, does not experience fear in situations that would terrify healthy individuals — being held at knifepoint, handling snakes and spiders, watching horror films. Her case demonstrated the amygdala's necessity for fear acquisition, though she retains normal emotional responses in other domains. The amygdala modulates the consolidation of emotionally charged memories through its projections to the hippocampus, explaining why emotionally arousing events are remembered more vividly than neutral ones.

## The Thalamus

Often called the brain's **relay station**, the **thalamus** is a paired, egg-shaped structure that sits near the center of the brain. Virtually all sensory information — visual, auditory, somatosensory, taste — passes through specific thalamic nuclei on its way to the cortex. The visual signal, for example, travels from the retina to the **lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN)** of the thalamus before reaching primary visual cortex.

But the thalamus is far more than a passive relay. It acts as a **gating mechanism**: during sleep, thalamic neurons fire in rhythmic bursts that actively block sensory information from reaching the cortex, contributing to the sensory disconnection of sleep. During wakeful attention, the thalamus facilitates transmission. It is intimately connected to the brainstem arousal systems and the cortex through reciprocal, looping circuits. Thalamic nuclei also interconnect cortical areas with each other and with the basal ganglia, making the thalamus a hub for large-scale brain network coordination.

## The Hypothalamus

The **hypothalamus** is a small (about 4 grams) but extraordinarily powerful structure at the base of the brain, just below the thalamus. It is the master regulator of **homeostasis** — the maintenance of stable body temperature, blood osmolarity, hunger, thirst, and sexual drive. Hypothalamic neurons monitor the blood directly, sampling glucose levels, hormone concentrations, and temperature, and respond by triggering behavioral drives (eating, drinking) or by signaling the pituitary gland to release hormones.

Through its control of the **pituitary gland**, the hypothalamus regulates the endocrine system: it releases hormones that govern the thyroid, adrenal glands, and gonads. The **suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)** of the hypothalamus serves as the brain's master circadian clock, receiving light information from the retina and coordinating [sleep-wake cycles](/human-body/brain/sleep) with the external environment.

## The Basal Ganglia

The **basal ganglia** are a group of interconnected subcortical nuclei — including the **striatum** (caudate + putamen), the **globus pallidus** (internal and external segments), the **subthalamic nucleus**, and the **substantia nigra** — that form a large loop with the cortex and thalamus. Their primary function relates to **action selection and motor control**, but they are also involved in procedural learning, habit formation, and motivated behavior.

The basal ganglia operate through **direct and indirect pathways** that, in simplified form, either facilitate or suppress motor programs. Dopamine from the substantia nigra modulates the balance: it generally promotes the direct pathway (facilitating movement) and inhibits the indirect pathway (suppressing competing movements). In Parkinson's disease, the loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra disrupts this balance, resulting in rigidity, bradykinesia (slowness of movement), and tremor.

The basal ganglia are also crucial for **habit formation**. As repeated behaviors are learned, their neural representations shift from the prefrontal cortex to the striatum, where they become automatic, requiring less conscious attention. This is efficient but inflexible — a mechanism that can underlie both healthy skill automatization and maladaptive behavioral habits in addiction.

## The Prefrontal Cortex

Though anatomically part of the frontal lobe cortex, the **prefrontal cortex (PFC)** deserves special mention here because of its dense interconnections with subcortical structures. The PFC is the seat of **executive function** — the deliberate, goal-directed control of behavior. Through its projections to the amygdala, it can suppress or modulate emotional responses. Through its connections to the basal ganglia and thalamus, it selects and coordinates action plans. The PFC is the last cortical region to complete myelination in development (around age 25) and the first to show atrophy in aging — it bookends the arc of human cognitive capacity.
