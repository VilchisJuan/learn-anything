## A Tale of Two Tissues

Slice a fresh human brain and the distinction is immediately apparent. The outer rind of the cerebral cortex appears dark gray-pink. Beneath it, the bulk of the brain looks white and glistening. These two colors reflect two fundamentally different types of neural tissue: **gray matter** and **white matter**. Understanding the distinction between them is essential for making sense of both normal brain function and what goes wrong in disease.

The naming is straightforwardly descriptive: gray matter gets its hue from the dense packing of neuronal cell bodies, which contain relatively little of the lipid that would make them reflective. White matter derives its color from **myelin**, the fatty insulating sheath produced by oligodendrocytes that wraps around axons and gives them an almost waxy appearance.

## Gray Matter: Where Processing Happens

Gray matter is where the brain's computational work takes place. It is composed primarily of:

- **Neuronal cell bodies (somata)** and their dendrites — the receiving and integrating components of neurons
- **Synapses** — the junctions where signals pass between neurons
- **Glial cells**, particularly astrocytes and microglia, which maintain the local environment
- Unmyelinated axons and small blood capillaries

In the cerebrum, gray matter forms the outermost layer: the **cerebral cortex**, a 2–4 mm thick sheet whose extraordinary folding packs roughly 2,500 cm² of computational surface into the human skull. But gray matter is not found only at the surface. Deep within the white matter lie clusters of gray matter called **subcortical nuclei** — including the basal ganglia, thalamus, and amygdala — that serve as relay and processing hubs for circuits connecting cortex to brainstem and spinal cord.

The cortex is organized into distinct functional areas. The **primary sensory and motor cortices** process and generate specific sensory/motor signals with topographic precision. **Association areas** — which constitute the majority of human cortex — integrate information from multiple sources and support higher cognitive functions such as language, planning, and abstract reasoning. The remarkable expansion of association cortex across primate evolution is one of the key anatomical features that distinguishes the human brain.

## White Matter: The Brain's Wiring

White matter is the brain's communication infrastructure. It consists almost entirely of **myelinated axon bundles** — long-range tracts that connect gray matter regions to one another. Without white matter, the cortex would be an island of isolated processors, unable to coordinate the widely distributed computations that underlie perception, action, and thought.

White matter tracts are classified by the directions they connect:

- **Commissural fibers** cross between the two hemispheres. The largest commissure is the **corpus callosum**, a massive band of roughly 200–300 million axons spanning the midline and enabling the two hemispheres to communicate. Cutting the corpus callosum produces the classic "split-brain" syndrome studied by Roger Sperry and Michael Gazzaniga.
- **Association fibers** connect cortical areas within the same hemisphere. Short U-fibers link adjacent gyri; long association tracts such as the **arcuate fasciculus** (connecting frontal and temporal language areas) span large distances.
- **Projection fibers** connect the cortex with subcortical structures and the spinal cord. The **internal capsule** is the critical bottleneck through which nearly all corticospinal motor fibers pass; a stroke here can cause devastating motor deficits.

## Imaging Gray and White Matter

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) has transformed the study of brain tissue organization. On a standard T1-weighted MRI, gray matter appears dark and white matter appears bright (the reverse of the gross anatomical convention). T2-weighted and FLAIR sequences are sensitive to abnormalities in white matter, showing areas of demyelination or edema as bright "lesions."

**Diffusion tensor imaging (DTI)** goes further, mapping the direction and integrity of white matter tracts based on the anisotropic diffusion of water molecules along axon bundles. DTI has revealed the large-scale architecture of the brain's white matter — its "connectome" — and has shown that white matter integrity correlates with cognitive performance, aging, and neurological disease.

## How Damage Differs

Damage to gray matter and white matter produces characteristically different deficits. Cortical gray matter damage typically produces highly specific, function-dependent impairments: a lesion in Broca's area disrupts speech production while leaving comprehension relatively intact; a lesion in the primary visual cortex causes a well-defined blind spot in the visual field.

White matter damage, by contrast, tends to produce deficits that are less anatomically precise but more broadly distributed — because white matter lesions disconnect networks rather than destroy local processors. This disconnection syndrome logic explains why **stroke** in the internal capsule causes widespread paralysis (the corticospinal tract, carrying all voluntary motor output, is compressed into a small area), and why **multiple sclerosis** — a white matter disease — produces a seemingly random, shifting pattern of deficits as different tracts come under attack.
