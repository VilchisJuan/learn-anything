## What Is a Neuron?

A **neuron** is a specialized cell designed to receive, process, and transmit electrical and chemical signals. While the brain contains many cell types, neurons are the units most directly responsible for computation and communication. They differ from most other cells in one crucial respect: they are electrically excitable. By rapidly changing the distribution of ions across their membrane, neurons generate brief electrical pulses — **action potentials** — that can travel rapidly across long distances without decaying in strength.

The human brain contains approximately 86 billion neurons, and each one may form anywhere from a few hundred to tens of thousands of synaptic connections with other neurons. The resulting network has a complexity that currently exceeds any human-engineered system.

## Anatomy of a Neuron

Despite enormous variety in shape and function, most neurons share the same basic structural organization.

### Dendrites

**Dendrites** are the neuron's input antennae — branching extensions that receive signals from other neurons. The word comes from the Greek for "tree," which aptly describes their branching morphology. In many neurons, dendritic branches are studded with small protrusions called **dendritic spines**, where the majority of excitatory synapses are located. The geometry of the dendritic tree determines how a neuron integrates signals from hundreds or thousands of incoming connections. Dendrites perform substantial computation locally; they are not simply passive wires.

### The Cell Body (Soma)

The **soma**, or cell body, is the metabolic center of the neuron. It contains the nucleus and most of the cell's organelles, including the ribosomes that manufacture proteins. The soma integrates the electrical currents arriving from dendrites and determines whether the total input is sufficient to trigger an action potential. This integration happens at a specialized region called the **axon hillock**, located at the junction between the soma and the axon.

### The Axon

The **axon** is the neuron's output cable — a single long process that carries action potentials away from the soma toward target cells. Axons range from less than a millimeter in length (for local interneurons) to over a meter (for spinal motor neurons innervating the feet). Many axons are wrapped in a fatty insulating sheath called **myelin**, produced by specialized glial cells. Myelin dramatically increases the speed at which signals travel, in a process called **saltatory conduction**, in which the action potential jumps from one gap in the myelin (a **node of Ranvier**) to the next.

### Axon Terminals and Synapses

At its far end, the axon branches into **axon terminals** (also called terminal boutons), which form **synapses** with the dendrites or cell bodies of target neurons, muscle cells, or glands. At a synapse, the arriving action potential triggers the release of chemical messengers called **neurotransmitters**, which diffuse across the synaptic cleft and bind to receptors on the receiving cell. This is the fundamental step by which one neuron communicates with another.

## Types of Neurons

The brain contains dozens of distinct neuron types, but they are commonly grouped into three broad functional categories.

### Sensory Neurons

**Sensory neurons** (also called afferent neurons) carry information from sensory receptors — in the skin, eyes, ears, nose, and internal organs — toward the central nervous system. Specialized sensory neurons convert physical stimuli (pressure, light, sound, temperature, chemicals) into electrical signals through a process called **transduction**.

### Motor Neurons

**Motor neurons** (efferent neurons) carry commands from the CNS out to effectors — primarily skeletal muscles and glands. **Upper motor neurons** originate in the cerebral cortex and descend to the spinal cord; **lower motor neurons** project from the spinal cord directly to muscle fibers. The disease amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) selectively destroys both types of motor neurons.

### Interneurons

**Interneurons** constitute the vast majority of neurons in the brain — over 99% by some estimates. They connect neurons to neurons within the CNS and are responsible for integrating information, producing complex outputs, and generating the brain's internal activity. They include both excitatory cells (typically glutamatergic pyramidal neurons) and inhibitory cells (typically GABAergic interneurons that regulate the activity of their neighbors).

## How Signals Travel

A neuron "fires" when the sum of excitatory and inhibitory inputs at the axon hillock reaches a threshold voltage (approximately −55 mV, from a resting potential of around −70 mV). At that point, an action potential is initiated: a rapid, stereotyped wave of electrical depolarization that travels down the axon at speeds of 0.5 to 120 meters per second, depending on axon diameter and myelination.

The action potential is **all-or-nothing**: it either fires at full strength or not at all. Information is encoded not in the amplitude of individual spikes but in their **frequency** (how many per second) and **timing patterns**. A neuron firing 100 times per second is conveying a stronger signal than one firing 5 times per second.
