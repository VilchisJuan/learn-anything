## What Is the Brain?

The human brain is the most complex organ known to science. Weighing roughly 1.4 kilograms and occupying the interior of the skull, it is the command center for virtually every thought, sensation, movement, and emotion you experience. It processes information arriving from the outside world, maintains the body's internal environment, stores decades of memories, and generates the subjective sense of being you. No other object of comparable size — a soft, wrinkled mass roughly the texture of firm tofu — has produced philosophy, music, mathematics, or the capacity to wonder about its own nature.

Understanding the brain is one of the defining scientific challenges of the twenty-first century. The field of neuroscience draws on biology, chemistry, physics, psychology, and computer science to piece together how roughly 86 billion neurons, interconnected by an estimated 100 trillion synapses, give rise to the full range of human experience.

## The Central and Peripheral Nervous Systems

The brain does not work in isolation. It is the centerpiece of the **central nervous system (CNS)**, which also includes the spinal cord. Together, the brain and spinal cord receive sensory information, integrate it, and coordinate responses. The CNS is the site of higher cognition, voluntary movement planning, and homeostatic regulation.

Beyond the CNS lies the **peripheral nervous system (PNS)**, a vast network of nerves that extends throughout the body. The PNS carries sensory signals toward the brain (afferent pathways) and motor commands away from it (efferent pathways). It is subdivided into the **somatic nervous system** — governing voluntary skeletal muscle movements — and the **autonomic nervous system**, which regulates involuntary processes such as heart rate, digestion, and breathing. The autonomic system is itself divided into the sympathetic ("fight-or-flight") and parasympathetic ("rest-and-digest") branches.

The brain relies on this peripheral infrastructure the way a city relies on roads. Without the PNS, the brain would have no way to sense the world or act on it.

## By the Numbers

The sheer scale of the brain is staggering. Approximately **86 billion neurons** populate the human brain, a figure often cited as "100 billion" — an estimate since revised downward by more precise stereological counting methods (Azevedo et al., 2009). But neurons make up only part of the story. Each neuron may form thousands of synaptic connections, producing an estimated **100 trillion synapses** in total — a number that rivals the count of stars in a thousand Milky Way galaxies.

Beyond neurons, the brain contains roughly an equal number of **glial cells** — a diverse family of support cells once thought to be mere packing material but now understood to be active participants in brain function (see [Glial Cells](/human-body/brain/glial-cells)).

The brain consumes approximately **20% of the body's total energy** despite accounting for only about 2% of its mass. This disproportionate metabolic demand reflects the ceaseless electrochemical signaling of neurons. Even at rest — doing nothing that feels like "thinking" — the brain hums with activity in a pattern of spontaneous fluctuations known as the default mode network.

## Major Divisions

At the broadest level, the brain can be divided into three main parts:

- The **cerebrum**, which accounts for about 85% of total brain weight and includes the familiar wrinkled cortex, is responsible for conscious thought, perception, language, and voluntary movement. Its surface — the cerebral cortex — is deeply folded into ridges (gyri) and grooves (sulci), an adaptation that packs a large surface area into a small skull.
- The **cerebellum** (Latin: "little brain"), perched at the back and bottom of the brain, coordinates movement, balance, and fine motor timing. It contains more than half of all neurons in the brain despite its smaller apparent size.
- The **brainstem**, connecting the cerebrum to the spinal cord, controls fundamental life-sustaining functions such as breathing, heart rate, and sleep-wake transitions.

These regions work in concert. No thought or action arises from a single brain area; almost everything involves distributed networks spanning multiple regions.

## Why It Matters

Studying the brain matters for reasons that go far beyond academic curiosity. Neurological and psychiatric disorders — including Alzheimer's disease, depression, epilepsy, stroke, and schizophrenia — collectively represent the leading cause of disability worldwide. Understanding how the healthy brain works is a prerequisite for understanding what goes wrong in disease and how to fix it.

The brain is also the lens through which all human knowledge is acquired. Every experiment ever run, every book ever written, every discovery ever made was produced by a brain. In a very real sense, neuroscience is the science that underlies all other sciences.
