## From Light to Perception

Vision is the dominant sense in humans, and the visual system consumes more cortical real estate than any other sensory modality — roughly one-third of the human cerebral cortex participates in visual processing to some degree. The journey from a pattern of light striking the retina to a conscious percept of a face, a word, or a moving object involves an ordered cascade of neural transformations distributed across the eye, the thalamus, and a hierarchy of cortical areas.

## The Retina

The visual system begins at the **retina**, a thin sheet of neural tissue lining the back of the eye. The retina contains two types of photoreceptors:

- **Rods**: approximately 120 million per retina, concentrated in the peripheral retina, optimized for low-light (scotopic) vision. They are exquisitely sensitive — a single photon can trigger a response — but convey no color information.
- **Cones**: approximately 6 million per retina, densely concentrated in the central **fovea**, responsible for high-acuity, color vision in bright light (photopic vision). Three cone types (S, M, L) are sensitive to different wavelengths, forming the basis of trichromatic color perception.

Photoreceptors synapse onto **bipolar cells**, which in turn synapse onto **retinal ganglion cells (RGCs)** — the retina's output neurons. Horizontal cells and amacrine cells mediate lateral interactions that sharpen contrast and detect motion even before the signal leaves the eye. The axons of all ~1.5 million RGCs bundle together at the **optic disk** (a blind spot, because there are no photoreceptors there) to form the **optic nerve**.

## The Optic Pathway: From Eye to Cortex

The two optic nerves meet at the **optic chiasm**, just below the hypothalamus, where a crucial rerouting occurs. Axons from the **nasal half** of each retina (which receive light from the temporal visual field) cross to the opposite hemisphere. Axons from the **temporal half** remain ipsilateral. The result: the left hemisphere receives visual information from the right visual field of both eyes, and vice versa.

From the optic chiasm, the pathways proceed as **optic tracts** to the **lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN)** of the thalamus. The LGN has six layers: layers 1 and 2 are the **magnocellular** (M) layers, receiving input from large, fast-conducting RGCs sensitive to motion and contrast. Layers 3–6 are the **parvocellular** (P) layers, receiving input from smaller RGCs tuned to fine detail and color. This M/P distinction is maintained through much of the visual hierarchy.

## Primary Visual Cortex (V1)

From the LGN, axons travel via the **optic radiations** to the **primary visual cortex (V1)**, located in the banks of the calcarine sulcus in the occipital lobe. V1 is retinotopically organized: adjacent parts of the visual field are represented by adjacent parts of V1, with the fovea (responsible for central, high-acuity vision) receiving a disproportionately large cortical representation — an instance of the general principle of cortical magnification.

V1 neurons are selective for specific stimulus features: orientation, spatial frequency, direction of motion, and eye of origin. Hubel and Wiesel showed that V1 neurons respond best to oriented edges and bars — not diffuse light — and demonstrated the columnar organization of these responses. Beyond V1 lie extrastriate areas (V2, V3, V4, V5/MT) that process increasingly complex features.

## The Two Visual Streams

One of the most influential organizational principles in vision neuroscience is the division of visual processing into two parallel streams beyond V1.

### The Dorsal ("Where") Stream

The **dorsal stream** runs from V1 upward into the **parietal lobe**, particularly into the posterior parietal cortex (area MT/V5 for motion, area MST for optic flow). It processes information about **location, motion, and spatial relationships** — answering the question "where is it?" and "how do I interact with it?" The dorsal stream guides reaching, grasping, and navigation. Damage to it can produce deficits in spatial perception and visually guided actions while leaving object recognition intact.

### The Ventral ("What") Stream

The **ventral stream** runs from V1 down and forward into the **temporal lobe**, through areas V2, V3, V4, and into the **inferotemporal cortex (IT)**. It extracts information about object identity — shape, color, texture — answering "what is it?" Area **V4** is involved in color processing. The **fusiform face area (FFA)** in the lateral fusiform gyrus responds selectively to faces. The **parahippocampal place area (PPA)** responds to scenes and spatial layouts. Damage to the ventral stream can produce **visual agnosia** — the inability to recognize objects despite intact visual acuity.

## Visual Field Defects and Localization

Because the visual pathway is anatomically organized, damage at specific points produces characteristic patterns of visual field loss that are valuable diagnostic tools. A lesion cutting one optic nerve causes blindness in that eye. A lesion at the optic chiasm interrupts the crossing nasal fibers and produces **bitemporal hemianopia** (loss of both peripheral, temporal visual fields) — a classic sign of a pituitary tumor pressing on the chiasm. A lesion behind the chiasm (in the optic tract or visual cortex) produces a **homonymous hemianopia** — loss of the same half of the visual field in both eyes.
