## Two Brains in One Skull

At first glance, the two halves of the human brain look like mirror images. The left and right **cerebral hemispheres** are symmetrical in gross anatomy, each containing the same four lobes, the same primary sensory and motor cortices, and the same major gyri and sulci. Yet they are not identical in function. The division of cognitive labor between hemispheres — **lateralization** — is one of the most fascinating organizing principles of the human brain.

Crucially, both hemispheres normally communicate through a massive white matter bridge called the **corpus callosum**, which contains 200–300 million axon fibers. Each hemisphere also receives information about and controls the opposite (contralateral) side of the body: the left hemisphere governs the right hand, processes sensory information from the right visual field, and vice versa. This **contralateral organization** is a conserved feature of vertebrate nervous systems, though its evolutionary rationale remains debated.

## The Corpus Callosum and Split-Brain Research

The most striking evidence for hemispheric specialization came from patients who had their corpus callosum surgically severed — a procedure called **corpus callosotomy**, once used to prevent severe epileptic seizures from spreading between hemispheres. These **split-brain** patients appeared normal in everyday life, but carefully controlled experiments by Roger Sperry and Michael Gazzaniga revealed the two hemispheres behaving as separate cognitive systems.

If a split-brain patient was shown a picture of an apple only to the left visual field (which projects to the right hemisphere), they could pick out the apple with their left hand but would verbally deny seeing anything — because language, residing in the left hemisphere, had no access to what the right hemisphere had seen. Conversely, words flashed to the right visual field (left hemisphere) could be spoken immediately.

This work, for which Roger Sperry received the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1981, established that each hemisphere has distinct cognitive specializations that are normally integrated through callosal communication. Gazzaniga's subsequent decades of research showed that the left hemisphere also harbors an "interpreter" — a tendency to generate explanatory narratives for actions whose actual causes it cannot access, offering a window into how the brain constructs a coherent sense of self.

## Language Lateralization

The most well-established hemisphere asymmetry is **language**. In approximately 96% of right-handed people and about 70% of left-handed people, language is lateralized to the **left hemisphere**. The key language areas — [Broca's area](/human-body/brain/language) in the left inferior frontal gyrus and Wernicke's area in the left posterior superior temporal gyrus — are almost entirely left-dominant.

This asymmetry was first discovered clinically in the nineteenth century. Paul Broca reported in 1861 that eight patients with speech production deficits all had damage to the same left frontal region. Carl Wernicke described language comprehension deficits from posterior temporal lesions in 1874. The fact that equivalent right hemisphere damage rarely produces comparable language deficits confirmed the left hemisphere's dominant role.

It should be noted, however, that the right hemisphere is not without linguistic function. It contributes to pragmatic aspects of language — understanding metaphor, humor, sarcasm, and narrative coherence — aspects that can be subtly impaired after right hemisphere damage.

## Spatial and Non-Verbal Processing

The right hemisphere shows relative dominance for several domains. **Spatial processing** — judging distances, perceiving three-dimensional structure, mental rotation, and navigation — is more right-lateralized. **Attention** also shows notable lateralization: the right hemisphere is thought to direct attention to both sides of space, while the left hemisphere attends mainly to the right side. This asymmetry explains why **hemispatial neglect** — the syndrome of failing to attend to one side of space — is far more severe and persistent after right hemisphere damage than left hemisphere damage. Patients with right parietal strokes may eat only from the right side of a plate, shave only the right side of their face, or fail entirely to acknowledge people standing to their left.

**Face recognition** and certain aspects of emotional processing also show right hemisphere dominance, though these lateralizations are less complete than language.

## Handedness and Brain Lateralization

**Handedness** is the most obvious behavioral manifestation of brain asymmetry. Approximately 89% of humans are right-handed, a figure that has remained stable across cultures and historical periods. Because the left hemisphere controls the right hand, the widespread preference for right-handedness is consistent with left-hemisphere dominance for skilled motor behavior.

The genetic basis of handedness is complex and not fully understood. Several genes have been implicated, including variants affecting the **LRRTM1** and **PCSK6** genes, which are involved in establishing left-right asymmetry during development. Interestingly, handedness and language lateralization are correlated but not perfectly coupled: about 4% of right-handers have right-hemisphere language, while about 27% of left-handers do.

## The Myth of "Left-Brain" and "Right-Brain" People

A popular but scientifically unsupported idea holds that people can be categorized as either "left-brained" (analytical, logical) or "right-brained" (creative, intuitive). This idea is a dramatic oversimplification. A 2013 analysis of resting-state fMRI data from over a thousand individuals (Nielsen et al.) found no evidence that people consistently use one hemisphere more than the other. Almost every cognitive function involves networks spanning both hemispheres. Creative processes engage frontal and parietal circuits on both sides; logical reasoning similarly draws on bilateral networks.

What is true is that certain specific functions show statistical lateralization at the population level — most notably language. What is not true is that individuals are characterized by a dominant hemisphere that shapes their personality or cognitive style.
