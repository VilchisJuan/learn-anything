## Building the Brain from Scratch

The human brain begins as a flat sheet of cells and, over approximately nine months of gestation and twenty-five or more years of postnatal life, becomes the most complex object in the known universe. This transformation is governed by a genetic program that coordinates cell division, migration, differentiation, synaptogenesis, and pruning — all while remaining sensitive to the environment. The result is a brain that is partly pre-specified by genes and partly sculpted by experience.

Understanding brain development is essential for understanding neurodevelopmental disorders, the nature of critical periods, and the trajectory of cognitive change from infancy through old age.

## Prenatal Development

### Neural Tube Formation

Development of the nervous system begins at approximately **week 3** of gestation, when a strip of ectoderm on the dorsal surface of the embryo thickens into the **neural plate**. By day 21–28, the neural plate folds and closes to form the **neural tube** — the precursor to the entire CNS. Failure of neural tube closure produces severe congenital defects: failure at the caudal end causes **spina bifida**; failure at the rostral end causes **anencephaly**. Adequate folate (vitamin B9) intake in early pregnancy dramatically reduces the risk of neural tube defects.

The rostral end of the neural tube expands into three primary brain vesicles: the **prosencephalon** (forebrain), **mesencephalon** (midbrain), and **rhombencephalon** (hindbrain). These further subdivide into five secondary vesicles by week 5, eventually giving rise to all brain structures.

### Neurogenesis

The majority of neurons in the cerebral cortex are generated between approximately **gestational weeks 10 and 20**, in a process of intense **neurogenesis** driven by progenitor cells in the **ventricular zone** (VZ) lining the neural tube's interior. These progenitors undergo two types of division: symmetric (expanding the progenitor pool) and asymmetric (producing one progenitor and one differentiating neuron). An intermediate progenitor zone (**subventricular zone, SVZ**) provides additional amplification, and is greatly expanded in humans compared to rodents — contributing to the cerebral cortex's relatively larger size and greater complexity in primates.

### Neuronal Migration

Newly born cortical neurons must travel enormous distances (relative to cell size) from the ventricular zone to their final positions in the cortical plate. Most neurons migrate radially outward along the elongated processes of **radial glia** — scaffold cells that span the thickness of the developing cortex and serve as guides. The cortex is built **inside-out**: the earliest-born neurons occupy the deepest layers (V and VI), while later-born neurons migrate past them to occupy progressively more superficial layers (IV, III, II). Disruptions to neuronal migration produce **lissencephaly** (smooth cortex, absent gyri) and other cortical malformations associated with intellectual disability and epilepsy.

Inhibitory interneurons take a different route: generated in the **ganglionic eminences** of the subcortical forebrain, they migrate tangentially across large distances to reach the cortex, arriving after excitatory neurons and integrating into local circuits.

### Synaptogenesis and Early Circuit Formation

Shortly before birth, axons begin forming synapses — a process that explodes after birth. **Synaptogenesis** peaks approximately at **18–24 months** in the human cerebral cortex, producing a synaptic density that exceeds that of the adult brain. This overabundance is not wasteful: it provides the raw material for experience-dependent selection.

## Postnatal Development

### Synaptic Pruning

Following the postnatal peak in synaptic density, the brain undergoes a prolonged period of **synaptic pruning** — selective elimination of weaker or unused connections. In the visual cortex, pruning is complete by late childhood; in the prefrontal cortex, it continues into the twenties. Pruning is guided by activity: synapses that are not reliably co-active with their postsynaptic partners are tagged with complement proteins (C1q, C3) and engulfed by microglia. This activity-dependent competition is the cellular basis of the classical critical period — synapses strengthened by appropriate visual experience survive; those deprived of input are eliminated.

Abnormalities in synaptic pruning have been implicated in schizophrenia (excessive pruning of PFC synapses during adolescence) and possibly autism spectrum disorder (insufficient pruning, leading to hyper-connected but less efficiently organized circuits).

### Myelination

**Myelination** of axons — the wrapping of oligodendrocyte processes around axons to form the myelin sheath — dramatically accelerates signal conduction and is therefore a major determinant of when a brain region becomes functionally effective. Myelination proceeds in a posterior-to-anterior, primary-to-association gradient: primary sensory and motor areas myelinate earliest; the prefrontal cortex last. Substantial myelination of frontal white matter tracts continues into the **mid-20s**, explaining the late maturation of executive function.

### The Adolescent Brain

Adolescence — roughly ages 10–25 — is a period of dramatic neural reorganization rather than simple maturation. The limbic/reward system (amygdala, nucleus accumbens, ventral striatum) matures early and is highly reactive during adolescence. The prefrontal executive control system matures late. This **temporal mismatch** produces the characteristic adolescent cognitive profile: heightened reward sensitivity, risk-taking, emotional reactivity, and peer influence susceptibility, combined with still-developing impulse control and long-term planning (see [Executive Function](/human-body/brain/executive-function)).

## Aging

At the other end of the lifespan, the brain undergoes a gradual and variable decline. **White matter integrity** declines measurably from the 40s onward, particularly in frontal and temporal tracts. **Hippocampal volume** decreases approximately 0.5–1% per year in normal aging, contributing to the mild episodic memory declines seen in healthy elderly adults. Prefrontal dopaminergic tone decreases, reducing working memory capacity and cognitive flexibility.

However, aging is not uniform. **Semantic memory** and vocabulary are often preserved or even improve with age. Individuals with high **cognitive reserve** — accumulated through education, occupational complexity, and cognitively stimulating leisure — show delayed onset of symptoms even in the presence of pathological changes.
