## When the Brain Breaks Down

The brain's extraordinary complexity carries a corresponding vulnerability. Subtle disruptions to molecular pathways, circuit dynamics, or structural integrity can produce disorders that range from devastating to barely perceptible. Understanding brain disorders matters not only for the suffering they cause — affecting over one billion people worldwide — but because pathology is one of the most powerful windows into normal brain function. Much of what we know about how the healthy brain works was discovered by studying what happens when specific systems fail.

## Neurodegenerative Diseases

### Alzheimer's Disease

**Alzheimer's disease** is the most prevalent neurodegenerative disorder, accounting for 60–70% of all dementia cases and affecting an estimated 50 million people globally. Its hallmark pathological features are **amyloid-beta plaques** (extracellular aggregates of misfolded Aβ peptide) and **neurofibrillary tangles** (intracellular aggregates of hyperphosphorylated tau protein), which were described by Alois Alzheimer in 1906. These pathologies spread in a characteristic sequence: tau tangles begin in the entorhinal cortex and hippocampus (explaining early memory deficits), while amyloid deposition begins in association cortices and spreads more diffusely.

The **amyloid cascade hypothesis** proposes that accumulation of Aβ (generated by abnormal cleavage of amyloid precursor protein) is the primary driver, with tau pathology and neuronal death downstream. Recent clinical trials of amyloid-clearing antibodies (lecanemab, donanemab) have shown modest but statistically significant slowing of cognitive decline in early-stage patients, providing the first pharmacological support for the amyloid hypothesis. Cholinergic degeneration (loss of basal forebrain neurons projecting to cortex) contributes to cognitive symptoms and underlies the partial efficacy of acetylcholinesterase inhibitors.

The [blood-brain barrier](/human-body/brain/blood-brain-barrier) is compromised early in Alzheimer's, and failure of [glymphatic clearance](/human-body/brain/sleep) during sleep may accelerate amyloid accumulation. Genetic risk factors include *APOE4* (the strongest common risk gene) and rare mutations in *APP*, *PSEN1*, and *PSEN2* that cause early-onset familial Alzheimer's.

### Parkinson's Disease

**Parkinson's disease** is characterized by the progressive loss of dopaminergic neurons in the **substantia nigra pars compacta** and their projections to the striatum. The cardinal motor features — tremor at rest, rigidity, bradykinesia, and postural instability — reflect the disruption of [basal ganglia motor circuits](/human-body/brain/motor-control) caused by dopamine depletion. The pathological hallmark is the **Lewy body**: an intracellular inclusion composed primarily of aggregated alpha-synuclein protein.

Treatment with **L-DOPA** (which crosses the [blood-brain barrier](/human-body/brain/blood-brain-barrier) and is converted to dopamine in surviving neurons) remains the most effective symptomatic therapy. **Deep brain stimulation (DBS)** of the subthalamic nucleus provides dramatic motor relief in selected patients by disrupting the pathologically synchronized beta oscillations that impede movement.

Heiko Braak's staging model proposes that Parkinson's pathology begins outside the brain — in the olfactory bulb and dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus — before progressing into the midbrain and eventually cortex. The early non-motor symptoms (anosmia, constipation, REM sleep behavior disorder) may precede motor symptoms by decades, offering potential windows for early intervention.

## Psychiatric Disorders

### Depression

**Major depressive disorder (MDD)** affects over 280 million people worldwide and is a leading cause of disability. The oldest neurobiological hypothesis — the **monoamine hypothesis** — proposes that depression results from insufficient monoamine (serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine) signaling, based on the observation that monoamine-depleting drugs cause depression and that monoamine-enhancing antidepressants relieve it. This hypothesis has proven too simple but remains partially valid.

More comprehensive models incorporate **HPA axis dysregulation** (elevated cortisol in many depressed patients, consistent with the hippocampal atrophy discussed in [Emotion](/human-body/brain/emotion)), **neuroinflammation** (elevated inflammatory markers in a substantial subset of depressed patients, and inflammatory cytokines that reduce monoamine synthesis), and **disrupted neuroplasticity** (reduced BDNF and impaired [adult neurogenesis](/human-body/brain/adult-neurogenesis)). Ketamine's rapid-acting antidepressant effect — producing remission within hours via NMDA receptor blockade — has highlighted glutamate signaling and [synaptic plasticity](/human-body/brain/plasticity) as therapeutic targets beyond monoamines.

### Schizophrenia

**Schizophrenia** is a complex psychiatric disorder affecting about 1% of the population, characterized by positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions, disorganized thought), negative symptoms (flat affect, social withdrawal, amotivation), and cognitive deficits (working memory, executive function, processing speed). The **dopamine hypothesis** — excess dopamine activity in mesolimbic circuits — is supported by the fact that all effective antipsychotics block dopamine D2 receptors, and by neuroimaging evidence of elevated dopamine synthesis capacity in the striatum. However, the dopamine hypothesis does not explain cognitive deficits or negative symptoms.

The **glutamate hypothesis** complements the dopamine account: administration of NMDA receptor antagonists (ketamine, PCP) produces a full schizophrenia-like syndrome in healthy volunteers, including positive and negative symptoms and cognitive deficits. Hypofunction of NMDA receptors on GABAergic interneurons may disinhibit dopamine circuits and disrupt the gamma oscillations and working memory supported by PFC parvalbumin interneurons.

## Epilepsy

**Epilepsy** is defined by recurrent, unprovoked seizures — episodes of abnormal, excessive, synchronized neuronal firing. Seizures arise when the normal balance between excitation and inhibition breaks down, either due to genetic mutations in ion channels or neurotransmitter receptors, structural brain abnormalities, or prior injury.

Seizures are classified by whether they begin focally (in one region) or with generalized onset. **Focal seizures** (formerly called partial) may remain local or secondarily generalize to involve both hemispheres. **Generalized seizures** — including absence seizures (brief, staring spells with 3 Hz spike-and-wave EEG discharges) and tonic-clonic seizures — involve both hemispheres from the outset. Treatment involves anticonvulsant drugs that target voltage-gated Na⁺ channels, GABA-A receptors, or calcium channels. Surgical resection of the seizure focus is curative in carefully selected drug-resistant cases.

## Stroke

**Stroke** is the second leading cause of death and a leading cause of disability worldwide. It occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is interrupted, causing ischemia and neuronal death within minutes. Two major types:

- **Ischemic stroke** (85% of cases): caused by thrombosis or embolism occluding an artery. Neurons in the core of the infarct die rapidly. Surrounding tissue — the **ischemic penumbra** — is at risk but may be salvageable with rapid reperfusion. Tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) and mechanical thrombectomy can restore flow and save penumbral tissue. The mantra "time is brain" (approximately 1.9 million neurons lost per minute of major stroke) underscores the urgency.
- **Hemorrhagic stroke** (15%): caused by rupture of a blood vessel, producing bleeding into or around brain tissue.

Recovery after stroke depends on [brain plasticity](/human-body/brain/plasticity), with perilesional and contralateral cortex reorganizing to partially compensate for lost function.

## Multiple Sclerosis

**Multiple sclerosis (MS)** is an autoimmune disease in which T-cells breach the [blood-brain barrier](/human-body/brain/blood-brain-barrier) and attack myelin sheaths in the CNS, producing plaques of demyelination (seen as white matter lesions on MRI). The resulting slowed or blocked nerve conduction produces the variable, episodic, and often accumulating deficits characteristic of the disease. Disease-modifying therapies that suppress or modulate the aberrant immune response have transformed MS from a rapidly disabling to a manageable chronic disease in many patients.

## Traumatic Brain Injury

**Traumatic brain injury (TBI)** ranges from mild concussion (temporary disruption of consciousness, headache, cognitive fog) to severe TBI (prolonged unconsciousness, devastating long-term deficits). Primary injury occurs at impact; secondary injury — edema, excitotoxicity, inflammatory cascades, and BBB breakdown — unfolds over hours to days and represents the target for neuroprotective interventions. Chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE), associated with repeated mild TBI (as in contact sports), involves progressive tau pathology and is linked to long-term cognitive and psychiatric decline.
