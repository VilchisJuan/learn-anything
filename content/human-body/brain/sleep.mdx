## Sleep Is Not Simply the Absence of Waking

For most of human history, sleep was understood as a passive state — the brain simply switching off for rest and recovery. The invention of the electroencephalogram (EEG) in the 1920s, and its application to sleeping subjects in the 1950s, overturned this view completely. The sleeping brain is not inactive; it cycles through a highly structured sequence of distinct states, each with characteristic neural activity patterns, physiological signatures, and functions.

## Sleep Stages: NREM and REM

A full night of sleep consists of 4–6 cycles, each lasting approximately 90 minutes. Each cycle contains both **non-rapid eye movement (NREM)** and **rapid eye movement (REM)** sleep.

### NREM Sleep

NREM sleep is subdivided into three stages.

**N1** (light sleep) is the transitional stage between wakefulness and sleep. The EEG shifts from alpha waves (8–12 Hz, characteristic of relaxed wakefulness with eyes closed) to theta waves (4–7 Hz). Muscle tone is reduced, and brief hypnic jerks — sudden muscle contractions that sometimes feel like falling — may occur. This stage is easily disrupted.

**N2** (intermediate sleep) is characterized by **sleep spindles** — brief bursts of 12–15 Hz oscillations generated by thalamocortical circuits — and **K-complexes** — large, sharp slow waves. N2 constitutes roughly 45–55% of total sleep time. Sleep spindles are generated by the thalamic reticular nucleus and are thought to reflect active sensory gating and memory consolidation processes.

**N3** (slow-wave sleep, SWS) is the deepest sleep stage, characterized by large-amplitude, low-frequency (0.5–4 Hz) **delta waves** on the EEG. It is hardest to awaken from. Growth hormone is secreted predominantly during SWS. Sleepwalking, sleep talking, and night terrors — disorders of incomplete arousal — occur during SWS. SWS is most abundant in the first half of the night.

### REM Sleep

**REM sleep** is characterized by an activated EEG resembling wakefulness, **rapid eye movements** beneath closed lids, and profound **muscle atonia** — almost all skeletal muscles are actively paralyzed by descending inhibition from the brainstem. This atonia prevents the physical enactment of dreams. (In REM sleep behavior disorder, this atonia fails, and sleepers act out their dreams with potentially injurious movements.)

REM sleep is also called **paradoxical sleep** because the brain is highly active but the body is almost completely still. Most vivid, narrative dreaming occurs during REM. Cholinergic neurons in the brainstem play a central role in generating REM, while noradrenergic and serotonergic neurons (which fire vigorously during waking) are almost completely silent during REM. REM sleep predominates in the second half of the night.

## The Architecture of Sleep: What Changes Across the Night

The proportion of SWS and REM shifts across the night. Early cycles contain long bouts of SWS and short REM periods; later cycles shift toward shorter SWS and progressively longer REM. This architecture has functional consequences: SWS-rich early sleep is particularly important for declarative memory consolidation, while REM-rich late sleep plays a greater role in procedural and emotional memory processing. Truncating sleep at either end disproportionately affects the corresponding functions.

## Functions of Sleep

Sleep's functions have been debated for decades, but three well-supported accounts now stand out.

### Memory Consolidation

The **memory consolidation** role of sleep is among the best-documented sleep functions. Declarative memories formed during waking are replayed in the hippocampus during NREM SWS — a process called **hippocampal replay** or **sharp-wave ripples** — and this replay appears to transfer memory representations from the hippocampus to the neocortex for long-term storage. Sleep spindles during N2 sleep facilitate this transfer by coordinating hippocampal and cortical activity. Studies consistently show that sleeping after learning — even a short nap — significantly improves later retention compared to an equivalent period of wakefulness.

### Glymphatic Clearance

A major discovery in 2013 by Maiken Nedergaard and colleagues revealed that the brain has its own waste-clearance system: the **glymphatic system**. During sleep — particularly SWS — the interstitial space between brain cells expands by up to 60%, allowing cerebrospinal fluid to flow through the brain via channels formed by astrocytic endfeet and flush out metabolic byproducts. Among the molecules cleared is **amyloid-beta**, the peptide that aggregates into plaques in Alzheimer's disease. Chronic sleep deprivation accelerates amyloid accumulation in animal models and in human neuroimaging studies, providing a compelling link between poor sleep and dementia risk.

### Synaptic Homeostasis

The **synaptic homeostasis hypothesis** (Tononi and Cirelli) proposes that waking experience progressively strengthens synapses throughout the brain through learning and activity. This synaptic "inflation" cannot be sustained indefinitely — it would eventually saturate neurons and consume unsustainable amounts of energy. Sleep, particularly SWS, serves to globally **downscale synaptic strength** back toward a sustainable baseline, selectively preserving stronger (better-learned) connections while reducing weaker ones. Evidence from molecular biology, electrophysiology, and structural imaging is broadly consistent with this hypothesis.

## Circadian Rhythm and the Suprachiasmatic Nucleus

The timing of sleep is governed by two overlapping systems. The **circadian process** is an approximately 24-hour endogenous rhythm generated by the **suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)** of the [hypothalamus](/human-body/brain/brain-regions-deep). SCN neurons contain a cell-autonomous molecular clock (CLOCK, BMAL1, PER, CRY proteins cycling in a transcription-translation feedback loop) and synchronize to the external light-dark cycle via direct retinal input. The SCN drives the release of **melatonin** from the pineal gland — rising in the evening as light decreases, peaking around 2–4 AM, and suppressed by morning light — which signals darkness and promotes sleep onset.

The **homeostatic process** (Process S) is a growing drive for sleep that accumulates as a function of time spent awake, driven largely by the buildup of **adenosine** in the basal forebrain. Caffeine works by blocking adenosine receptors, suppressing this homeostatic drive without actually reducing the need for sleep.
