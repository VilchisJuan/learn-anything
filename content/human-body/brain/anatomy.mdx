## The Cerebral Cortex and Its Four Lobes

The most visible feature of the human brain is the deeply folded outer sheet of the cerebrum known as the **cerebral cortex**. Spread flat, the cortex would cover roughly 2,500 square centimeters — about the area of a pillowcase. Evolution solved the problem of fitting this large surface into a fixed skull by folding it into ridges called **gyri** and furrows called **sulci**. Deeper, more prominent furrows are called **fissures**.

The cortex is divided into four major lobes, each named after the skull bone that overlies it.

### The Frontal Lobe

Occupying the front third of the cerebral hemisphere, the frontal lobe is the seat of voluntary movement, planning, decision-making, and social behavior. Its posterior border is defined by the **central sulcus**, behind which sits the primary motor cortex — a strip of cortex that maps to body regions through the famous motor homunculus. The most anterior portion, the **prefrontal cortex**, is disproportionately enlarged in humans and underlies the executive functions that define much of human cognition.

### The Parietal Lobe

Immediately behind the central sulcus, the parietal lobe processes somatosensory information — touch, pain, temperature, and proprioception (the sense of body position). Just behind the central sulcus lies the **primary somatosensory cortex**, which maintains a topographic map of the body surface analogous to the motor homunculus. The parietal lobe also plays a central role in spatial perception, attention, and the integration of information from different sensory modalities.

### The Temporal Lobe

Running along the sides of the brain (roughly above the ears), the temporal lobes handle auditory processing, language comprehension, and the recognition of faces and objects. The **primary auditory cortex** sits on the superior temporal plane. Deep within the temporal lobe lie the **hippocampus** and **amygdala** — structures critical for memory and emotion, respectively.

### The Occipital Lobe

At the rear of the brain, the occipital lobe is dedicated almost entirely to visual processing. The **primary visual cortex (V1)** receives input from the eyes via the thalamus and begins the complex hierarchical processing that eventually produces conscious sight.

## The Cerebellum

Tucked beneath the posterior cerebrum, the **cerebellum** (Latin for "little brain") accounts for only about 10% of total brain volume but harbors more than 50% of all neurons in the brain — a testament to its enormous internal complexity. The cerebellum does not initiate movement, but it is indispensable for fine-tuning it. It compares intended movements with actual movements and sends corrective signals in real time, enabling smooth, coordinated motor control. Damage to the cerebellum produces characteristic symptoms: ataxia (unsteady gait), dysmetria (misjudging distances), and intention tremor.

Increasingly, research has implicated the cerebellum in cognitive and language functions as well, though its primary role remains motor coordination.

## The Brainstem

The brainstem is the stalk-like structure connecting the cerebrum to the spinal cord. It is composed of three parts, each with distinct functions.

### Midbrain

The topmost part of the brainstem, the **midbrain** (mesencephalon), contains the superior and inferior colliculi — structures involved in visual and auditory reflexes, respectively — as well as the **substantia nigra**, a pigmented region critical for dopamine production and motor control. Loss of neurons in the substantia nigra is the defining pathological feature of Parkinson's disease.

### Pons

The **pons** (Latin: "bridge") sits in the middle of the brainstem and serves as a relay station between the cerebrum and the cerebellum. It also contains nuclei that regulate breathing rhythm, eye movements, and facial sensation and expression. Several cranial nerve nuclei reside here.

### Medulla Oblongata

The lowest part of the brainstem, the **medulla**, contains the vital centers for autonomic control: heart rate, blood pressure, breathing, and reflexes such as swallowing and vomiting. Injury to the medulla is often fatal because it disrupts these life-sustaining functions.

## The Meninges

The brain is not simply floating free inside the skull. It is enveloped by three protective membranes collectively called the **meninges**.

The outermost and toughest layer is the **dura mater** (Latin: "tough mother"), a thick fibrous sheet that lines the inside of the skull. The middle layer, the **arachnoid mater**, is a delicate web-like membrane. Between the arachnoid and the innermost layer, the **pia mater**, lies the **subarachnoid space**, which is filled with **cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)**. The pia mater closely follows every contour of the brain's surface.

CSF cushions the brain against mechanical shocks, removes metabolic waste, and maintains a stable ionic environment. It is produced primarily by the **choroid plexus** within the four brain ventricles and circulates continuously before being reabsorbed into the venous system.

Inflammation of the meninges — **meningitis** — is a medical emergency that can be caused by bacterial or viral infection. A collection of blood in the subarachnoid space (**subarachnoid hemorrhage**) is a life-threatening stroke variant often caused by rupture of an arterial aneurysm.
