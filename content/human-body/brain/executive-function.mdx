## The Brain's CEO

The analogy is imperfect but instructive: if the brain were a corporation, the **prefrontal cortex (PFC)** would be the executive suite. It does not perform sensory processing, motor execution, or memory storage — other regions handle those — but it directs, coordinates, plans, and monitors the systems that do. It determines which information is relevant and held in mind, which impulses are suppressed, which strategies are maintained or switched, and which values guide decisions. Together, these capacities are called **executive function**.

Executive function is not a single ability. It is a family of related but dissociable capacities, each with distinct neural correlates within and around the prefrontal cortex, each with its own developmental trajectory, and each vulnerable to disruption by different neurological and psychiatric conditions.

## Working Memory

**Working memory** is the most fundamental component of executive function — the online maintenance and manipulation of information over short periods. The classic model (Baddeley, detailed in [Memory](/human-body/brain/memory)) distinguishes the phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad, and central executive.

The neural substrate of working memory is principally the **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC)**. PFC neurons "bridge the temporal gap" by sustaining their firing during the delay period between presentation of a stimulus and the response that must be based on it — a property called **persistent activity** or delay-period firing. This sustained representation of "what must be remembered and used" is the neural implementation of keeping information "in mind."

Working memory capacity is strongly predictive of performance on intelligence tests, academic achievement, and the ability to follow complex instructions. It is reliably reduced in schizophrenia (where dlPFC dopamine dysregulation is thought to impair sustained PFC activity), ADHD, and normal aging.

## Inhibitory Control

**Inhibitory control** is the ability to suppress dominant, automatic, or prepotent responses when they are inappropriate. It prevents reflexive actions from overriding deliberate ones, suppresses distracting information, and stops ongoing behavior when a goal changes. Without inhibitory control, behavior becomes stimulus-driven and impulsive.

The classic behavioral probe is the **Stroop task**: subjects are presented with color words (e.g., the word "RED" printed in blue ink) and asked to name the ink color while ignoring the word. The powerful, automatic tendency to read the word creates a conflict that requires inhibitory control to resolve. Patients with frontal lobe damage perform poorly on this and related tasks.

Inhibitory control involves the **right inferior frontal gyrus** and the **anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)**, which detects conflict and signals the need for control. The ACC, sandwiched between the frontal lobes in the cingulate sulcus, is active whenever a task requires overriding a strong competing response — suggesting it acts as a conflict monitor that triggers recruitment of prefrontal control.

## Cognitive Flexibility and Set-Shifting

**Cognitive flexibility** is the ability to shift strategies or perspectives in response to changing task demands — to update the "mental set" governing ongoing behavior. It involves disengaging from one framework and switching to another, suppressing the now-irrelevant former approach.

A common laboratory measure is the **Wisconsin Card Sorting Task (WCST)**: subjects must sort cards according to a rule (color, shape, or number of symbols) that changes without warning after a series of correct sorts. Patients with prefrontal cortex damage show pronounced **perseveration** — continuing to sort by the old rule even after feedback signals that it is wrong. This striking inability to shift set reveals the PFC's role in updating behavior when circumstances change.

Cognitive flexibility depends on a balance between dopamine and norepinephrine modulation in the PFC — not too little (which produces rigidity) and not too much (which produces impulsivity and distractibility). The PFC operates best at an optimal level of monoamine tone that can be shifted by stress, fatigue, and drugs.

## Decision-Making: Ventromedial and Orbitofrontal Cortex

While the dlPFC supports working memory and abstract rule use, the **ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC)** and **orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** are more directly involved in **value-based decision-making** — assigning worth to outcomes and guiding choices accordingly.

The crucial evidence comes from patients with vmPFC lesions, studied by Antonio Damasio and colleagues. Despite normal intelligence, reasoning, and explicit knowledge of decision-making rules, these patients make chronically poor life decisions — failing at jobs, relationships, and financial planning. In the **Iowa Gambling Task**, healthy subjects learn implicitly to avoid high-risk decks of cards (which deliver large rewards but even larger occasional penalties), while vmPFC patients continue to choose them even after learning the odds.

Damasio's **somatic marker hypothesis** proposes that the vmPFC stores associations between past choices and their emotional/physiological consequences, generating "gut feelings" that guide future decisions. Damage to the vmPFC removes these fast, affectively weighted signals, leaving decision-making technically intact but practically catastrophic.

## Development of the Prefrontal Cortex

Among all cortical regions, the PFC is the last to mature. **Myelination** of PFC connections continues into the mid-20s. **Synaptic pruning** of PFC circuits is ongoing through adolescence. This late maturation has profound consequences for behavior during adolescence — a period characterized by heightened risk-taking, reward-seeking, emotional reactivity, and diminished impulse control.

The adolescent brain has a maturationally advanced limbic/reward system (the striatum and amygdala) and a still-developing prefrontal regulatory system. The resulting imbalance — strong motivation with weak top-down control — has been described as a "dual systems" model and provides a neurobiological framing for many characteristically adolescent behaviors. Only around age 25 does PFC-mediated executive control reach full adult maturity.
