## Overturning a Dogma

For much of the twentieth century, one of the most firmly held beliefs in neuroscience was that the adult mammalian brain cannot generate new neurons. The idea originated with Santiago Ramón y Cajal, who wrote in 1913: "In the adult centers the nerve paths are something fixed, ended, immutable. Everything may die, nothing may be regenerated." Given that neurons are among the most structurally complex and metabolically demanding cells in biology, and given that most neurons in the brain are indeed present from early development and maintained throughout life, this seemed reasonable.

The dogma began cracking in the 1960s, when Joseph Altman and Gopal Das published autoradiographic studies showing evidence of new cell division in the rat dentate gyrus and olfactory bulb. But their results were largely ignored or disputed. It took the development of the **BrdU (bromodeoxyuridine) labeling method** in the 1990s — a technique that incorporates a synthetic thymidine analog into dividing cells' DNA during replication, permanently marking newly born cells — to produce unambiguous and widely accepted evidence of adult neurogenesis in rodents, and eventually in other mammals including primates and, provisionally, humans.

## Where Does Neurogenesis Occur?

In the adult mammalian brain, new neurons are generated in two well-established **neurogenic niches**:

### The Dentate Gyrus of the Hippocampus

The **dentate gyrus (DG)** is the primary input region of the hippocampus, receiving signals from the entorhinal cortex via the perforant path. New granule cells are continuously born from a population of radial glia-like stem cells (type 1 cells) in the **subgranular zone (SGZ)**, a thin strip of cells between the granule cell layer and the hilus. Newly born cells pass through a sequence of intermediate progenitor stages over several weeks before maturing into functionally integrated granule neurons with dendrites reaching into the molecular layer and axons projecting to CA3. During this maturation period — particularly in the first four to six weeks — new neurons are more excitable and show enhanced LTP compared to mature neurons, suggesting a special role in certain forms of learning.

### The Olfactory Bulb

New neurons are also generated in the **subventricular zone (SVZ)** lining the lateral ventricles and migrate along the **rostral migratory stream** to the olfactory bulb, where they differentiate into interneurons. This pathway is robust in rodents but appears much reduced or functionally marginal in the adult human brain.

## Functional Roles

### Pattern Separation

The most supported functional role for adult-born dentate granule neurons is **pattern separation** — the ability to distinguish between similar but overlapping inputs, encoding them as distinct representations. The dentate gyrus is thought to perform "orthogonalization" of incoming inputs before passing them to CA3, reducing interference between similar memories. Adult-born neurons, with their period of enhanced excitability, appear to contribute disproportionately to pattern separation. Ablation of adult neurogenesis (by X-irradiation or genetic means) impairs pattern separation performance in behavioral tasks.

### Anxiety and Mood

Adult hippocampal neurogenesis has been consistently linked to anxiety regulation and the response to antidepressant treatments. All major classes of antidepressants — SSRIs, SNRIs, tricyclics, and even ketamine — increase neurogenesis rates in rodents. Crucially, in some mouse models, ablating neurogenesis blocks the behavioral effects of SSRIs, suggesting that new neuron production is required for antidepressant action. Chronic stress — via elevated cortisol — suppresses neurogenesis, while exercise powerfully stimulates it. These findings frame neurogenesis as a homeostatic mechanism that buffers mood and stress reactivity, though whether the same relationship holds for human antidepressant response remains an active area of investigation.

## The 2018 Controversy in Human Neurogenesis

While adult hippocampal neurogenesis is well established in rodents, its extent and significance in adult humans has been vigorously debated. Two landmark papers published in the same year (2018) reached opposite conclusions. Sorrells and colleagues (Alvarez-Buylla lab) reported finding essentially no new neurons in the adult human hippocampus using one set of immunohistochemical markers, concluding that human adult neurogenesis is negligible or absent. Boldrini and colleagues (Peterson lab), using complementary methods, reported robust evidence of new neuron production continuing into old age.

The discrepancy likely reflects differences in tissue fixation protocols, postmortem intervals, and choice of cell markers — all of which dramatically affect the detectability of immature neurons. The field has not yet resolved this controversy, though more recent studies using improved methods have tended to support the existence of adult neurogenesis in the human hippocampus, albeit at lower rates than in rodents.

## Factors That Regulate Neurogenesis

Several factors have been reliably shown to increase or decrease adult hippocampal neurogenesis in animal models:

**Increases neurogenesis**: aerobic exercise (the most robust pro-neurogenic stimulus known), environmental enrichment (novelty, social interaction, exploration), learning, caloric restriction, antidepressants, BDNF, and estrogens.

**Decreases neurogenesis**: chronic stress, sleep deprivation, social isolation, aging, alcohol, opioids, and inflammation.

The strong effect of exercise on neurogenesis — mediated partly by BDNF release from contracting muscles and partly by increased hippocampal blood flow — has made exercise a subject of considerable interest in both aging research and the prevention of cognitive decline.
