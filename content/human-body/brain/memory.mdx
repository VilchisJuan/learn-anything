## Memory Is Not One Thing

When we speak of "memory," we refer loosely to an enormous range of cognitive capacities: remembering a phone number for thirty seconds, recalling what you had for breakfast, knowing that Paris is the capital of France, learning to ride a bicycle, flinching at a sound that once preceded pain. These capacities feel similar because they all draw on past experience, but they involve distinct neural systems that can be independently damaged. Understanding memory means understanding this diversity.

## Working Memory

**Working memory** is the brain's workspace — the system that holds a small amount of information in an active, accessible state for use in ongoing cognitive tasks. When you mentally multiply 23 by 7, or hold a phone number in mind while dialing it, you are using working memory. It is not merely a passive store but an active, capacity-limited process that can manipulate as well as hold information.

Alan Baddeley's influential model divides working memory into three components: the **phonological loop** (holding verbal/acoustic information), the **visuospatial sketchpad** (holding visual and spatial information), and the **central executive** (directing attention and coordinating the other components). The prefrontal cortex — particularly the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex — is the primary neural substrate, with contributions from posterior cortical areas depending on the modality of the material. Working memory capacity correlates strongly with measures of general intelligence and academic achievement.

## Long-Term Memory: Explicit and Implicit

Long-term memory is conventionally divided into **explicit (declarative)** and **implicit (non-declarative)** systems.

### Explicit Memory

**Explicit memory** consists of memories that can be consciously recalled and verbally described.

- **Episodic memory**: Memory for specific events ("personal history"), including their time, place, and emotional context. Remembering your first day at school, or what you did last Saturday, draws on episodic memory. It has been described as "mental time travel" — the ability to re-experience past events.
- **Semantic memory**: Knowledge of facts about the world, abstracted from the specific context in which they were learned. Knowing that neurons communicate via synapses, or that the Amazon is the world's largest rainforest, is semantic memory.

Both forms of explicit memory depend critically on the **hippocampus** for encoding and early consolidation, but are eventually stored in distributed neocortical networks. The hippocampus is not the permanent storage site — it is the index and consolidator.

### The Case of Patient H.M.

The most famous case in memory neuroscience is that of Henry Molaison (known during his lifetime as "H.M."), who underwent bilateral medial temporal lobe removal in 1953. He retained memories from before his surgery (retrograde memory was partially preserved) but could form no new explicit memories (a profound anterograde amnesia). He could not recognize his doctors from one day to the next, could not learn new facts, and could not remember events even minutes after they occurred.

H.M.'s case established that the [hippocampus](/human-body/brain/brain-regions-deep) is necessary for **encoding new explicit memories**, and it dissociated explicit memory from other forms of learning that H.M. retained normally — particularly implicit memory.

### Implicit Memory

**Implicit memory** operates outside conscious awareness and is revealed by changes in performance rather than direct recall.

- **Procedural memory**: Motor skills and cognitive habits — riding a bicycle, typing, reading music. Depends on the basal ganglia, motor cortex, and cerebellum. H.M. could learn new motor skills even though he had no memory of the learning sessions.
- **Priming**: Exposure to a stimulus facilitates subsequent processing of related stimuli, without conscious recollection of the prior exposure. Depends on neocortical processing areas.
- **Classical conditioning**: Learned associations between neutral stimuli and emotionally or reflexively significant events. Emotional conditioning involves the **amygdala**; skeletal muscle conditioning involves the cerebellum.

## Encoding, Consolidation, and Retrieval

Memory has three stages:

**Encoding** is the initial formation of a memory trace. It is strongly influenced by attention (unattended information is poorly encoded), emotional significance (the amygdala enhances encoding of emotionally arousing material), and processing depth (semantic/meaningful processing leads to stronger encoding than shallow processing).

**Consolidation** is the process by which newly formed, labile memories are stabilized. Cellular consolidation involves synaptic strengthening through [long-term potentiation](/human-body/brain/synaptic-transmission). Systems consolidation involves a gradual transfer of memory representations from the hippocampus to the neocortex over weeks to years. [Sleep](/human-body/brain/sleep) is critical for this process — memory replay during slow-wave sleep and REM sleep strengthens memory traces.

**Retrieval** is the reactivation of a stored memory. Retrieval is not like reading from a hard drive — it is a reconstructive process, subject to distortion, suggestion, and interference. The act of retrieval itself modifies the memory (reconsolidation), which has important implications for understanding eyewitness testimony and the treatment of traumatic memories.

## Synaptic Basis of Memory

The synaptic changes underlying memory storage involve **long-term potentiation (LTP)** — the persistent strengthening of a synapse following repeated activation. The key molecular mechanism involves NMDA receptors as coincidence detectors: when presynaptic glutamate release coincides with postsynaptic depolarization, Ca²⁺ enters through NMDA channels, triggering a cascade that inserts more AMPA receptors and strengthens the synapse. This is explored in detail in [Brain Plasticity](/human-body/brain/plasticity).
