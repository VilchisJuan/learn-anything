## What Is an Ammonite?

Ammonites were marine invertebrates — soft-bodied animals without a backbone that lived in the sea. They are **cephalopod mollusks**, the same animal group that today includes squids, octopuses, and nautiluses. Unlike their modern relatives, ammonites are **entirely extinct**, having disappeared 66 million years ago.

Their most recognizable feature is their **coiled shell**: a beautiful logarithmic spiral divided into chambers. The animal itself lived inside only the outermost, largest chamber — the rest was used for buoyancy control, like a submarine's ballast tanks.

The giant *Parapuzosia seppenradensis* wasn't a rare freak — complete specimens are known from Germany. Some palaeontologists estimate the animal inside may have weighed over 200 kg.

## Where They Fit in the Tree of Life

Ammonites didn't evolve in isolation. They belong to the **cephalopods** — a lineage of intelligent, active predators that has existed for over 500 million years. Squids and octopuses are their distant cousins; nautiluses are their closest living relative.

<CephalopodTree />

The key difference between ammonites and nautiloids is the **siphuncle** — the tube that runs through the chambers. In nautiloids it runs through the centre; in ammonites it runs along the outer wall. This distinction, along with their suture patterns, is what defines them as a separate group.

## When Did They Live?

Ammonoids first appeared in the **Early Devonian** (~400 million years ago) and survived until the **end of the Cretaceous** (66 million years ago) — that's **334 million years** of continuous existence. Longer than the time from the first dinosaurs to today.

They survived **three major mass extinctions**, each time recovering and diversifying more spectacularly than before. They were killed only by the most violent event of the last 250 million years: the Chicxulub asteroid impact.

## Why Are They So Important?

**As geological clocks.** Ammonite species evolved quickly and spread worldwide — making them perfect **index fossils**. Find the right ammonite and you can date the rock layer to within a million years. The Jurassic and Cretaceous time scales are subdivided almost entirely using ammonite species zones.

**As a window into evolution.** Their 334-million-year fossil record shows continuous evolutionary change — the most complete, best-preserved record of any major animal group. Darwin cited cephalopods extensively in discussing evolution.

**As proof that life rebounds.** Three times they were nearly annihilated. Three times they came back stronger. Their story is one of the most powerful demonstrations of evolution's resilience in all of natural history.

## What Did They Actually Look Like?

The shell is what we find as fossils. The living animal extended out of the front opening (**aperture**) and looked something like a nautilus crossed with a squid:

- **Tentacles** (probably 8–10) for seizing prey
- **Eyes** — likely large and capable, as with modern cephalopods
- **A siphon** (hyponome) for jet propulsion
- **A beak** (aptychus) for biting

They swam by jet propulsion — sucking water into a mantle cavity and expelling it forcefully — and could hover at any depth by adjusting gas in their shell's chambers.
