## Why Ammonites Make Great Fossils

Not all organisms preserve well as fossils. Ammonites had several features that made them ideal candidates:

1. **Hard shells** — made of aragonite (a form of calcium carbonate), shells resist rapid decomposition
2. **Marine habitat** — sediments accumulate rapidly in shallow seas, quickly burying shells
3. **Enormous abundance** — tens of thousands of species, billions of individuals, over 334 million years
4. **Global distribution** — found on every continent, including Antarctica

The result: ammonites are among the **most commonly found fossils worldwide**.

## How Does an Ammonite Become a Fossil?

### Stage 1: Death and Burial

When an ammonite died, its soft body decayed rapidly within weeks to months. The hard shell sank to the seafloor (or floated briefly if gas remained in the chambers). If conditions were right — fine-grained sediment, calm water, rapid burial — the shell was covered before it could be destroyed.

### Stage 2: Lithification

Layers of sediment accumulated over thousands to millions of years. The weight compressed the sediment, driving out water and converting it to rock. The shell underwent chemical changes as groundwater percolated through.

### Stage 3: Mineralogical Replacement

Most commonly, the original shell material is either altered or completely replaced by new minerals. This process — **diagenesis** — creates the spectacular variety of ammonite preservation we find today.

The rarest and most prized specimens preserve the original aragonite shell with thin-film iridescence (ammolite) or are completely replaced by rainbow opal. But even the most common calcite replacement can preserve exquisite detail of suture lines and ornamentation.

## Concretions — Nature's Fossil Cases

Many of the best-preserved ammonites are found inside **concretions** — hard, roughly spherical masses of calcium carbonate or iron carbonate that formed around the shell early in burial, before compaction could crush it.

Cracking open a concretion can reveal a perfectly **three-dimensional ammonite**, often with the original shell architecture intact. Famous concretion localities include:

- **Lyme Regis, Dorset, UK** — Jurassic Blue Lias mudstone concretions with *Asteroceras* and *Arietites*
- **Posidonia Shale, Germany** — pyrite *Harpoceras* in stunning 3D, sometimes with soft tissue traces
- **Erfoud, Morocco** — large concretions with multiple coexisting Devonian and Cretaceous species

## Where to Find Ammonite Fossils

### United Kingdom
- **Yorkshire coast** (Robin Hood's Bay, Whitby): Jurassic Lias shales — pyrite *Dactylioceras* and *Hildoceras*. Local legend called them "snake stones" — petrified snakes cursed by St. Hild.
- **Dorset coast** (Charmouth, Lyme Regis): Jurassic Blue Lias — *Asteroceras*, *Arietites*, *Psiloceras*; many accessible on the beach after storms.
- **Oxfordshire quarries**: Large *Perisphinctes* and *Cardioceras* from Oxfordian limestones.

### Germany
- **Solnhofen limestone, Bavaria**: Exceptionally preserved Jurassic fauna alongside *Archaeopteryx*.
- **Holzmaden, Baden-Württemberg**: World-famous pyrite ammonites in the Posidonia Shale; some with preserved soft tissue traces.

### Morocco
- **Erfoud, Drâa-Tafilalet**: Both Devonian goniatites and Cretaceous ammonites; polished cut-sections sold worldwide. The red and grey limestone makes spectacular display pieces.

### Madagascar
- **Mahajanga Basin**: Spectacularly preserved Cretaceous *Cleoniceras* with original iridescent shell; sometimes in nodules with complete 3D preservation.

### Canada
- **Alberta (Bearpaw Formation)**: Ammolite — iridescent Cretaceous *Placenticeras* in commercial quantities. Legally mined near Lethbridge by Korite International.

### United States
- **Montana and South Dakota**: Late Cretaceous ammonites from the Western Interior Seaway; large *Placenticeras* and heteromorphs.
- **Texas (Greenhorn Formation)**: Large *Coilopoceras* and *Sphenodiscus*.

## Ammonites as Index Fossils

The most scientifically important property of ammonites is their use in **biostratigraphy** — using fossils to date rocks.

Index fossils must meet three criteria:
1. **Wide geographic distribution** — found on multiple continents simultaneously
2. **Narrow time range** — evolved and went extinct quickly, so their presence tightly constrains the age
3. **Easily identifiable** — distinctive enough to recognise from fragments

Ammonites meet all three. They evolved rapidly (new species appearing every 200,000–1,000,000 years), spread across global oceans quickly, and have distinctive shells.

The **Jurassic** and **Cretaceous** periods are subdivided using **ammonite biozones** — time intervals defined by the first and last appearances of specific ammonite species. Over 70 biozones are recognised in the Jurassic of northwest Europe alone, each representing ~500,000–1,500,000 years.

Find the right ammonite and a specialist can tell you **within a million years** when it lived.

## How to Identify What You've Found

When you find an ammonite, record these features for identification:

- **Diameter and whorl width** — how wide is each whorl relative to the whole?
- **Umbilicus** — is the centre open (evolute) or closed (involute)?
- **Ribbing** — fine or coarse? Do ribs bifurcate (split at a tubercle)?
- **Tubercles or spines** — any bumps or projections?
- **Keel** — is there a ridge along the outer edge (venter)?
- **Sutures** — simple angular, serrated, or highly complex?

## What to Do if You Find One

In most countries, collecting from loose beach material is legal. Key rules:

- **UK**: Do not hammer fossils directly from cliff faces — illegal in many SSSI areas and dangerous. Collect from loose material only.
- **USA federal land**: Collection requires a permit; commercial collecting is strictly regulated.
- **Exceptional specimens**: Contact your local natural history museum. Many landmark specimens came from amateur collectors — Mary Anning, who discovered the first *Ichthyosaurus* and *Plesiosaurus*, also found many ammonites near Lyme Regis.

Ammonites connect us directly to vanished ocean worlds. Hold one in your hand and you are touching something that last saw daylight 66 to 400 million years ago.
