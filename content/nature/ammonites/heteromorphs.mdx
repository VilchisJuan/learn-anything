## The Rule-Breakers

For 300 million years, ammonites kept to a single successful body plan: a planispiral coiled shell, each whorl overlapping the previous. Then, in the Triassic and especially the Cretaceous, something changed. Groups of ammonites began to **uncoil** — producing some of the strangest shell forms in the history of animal life.

These are the **heteromorphs** (from Greek: *hetero* = different, *morph* = shape). They include straight rods, hooks, open spirals, helical screws, and the infamous knotted tangle of *Nipponites*.

## Was Uncoiling Random?

Uncoiling is not random, nor is it an evolutionary dead end. Several key observations:

1. **It happened repeatedly and independently.** Heteromorphs evolved in at least 15 separate lineages across geological time. This is convergent evolution on a grand scale — the same departure from coiling arising again and again, suggesting it was repeatedly advantageous.

2. **It accelerated toward the K-Pg boundary.** Heteromorph diversity peaked in the Late Cretaceous. As conditions changed and ecological niches shifted, the selective pressure for conventional coiling weakened.

3. **Heteromorphs were successful in their own terms.** Baculites became one of the most abundant large invertebrates of the Western Interior Seaway. *Scaphites* are among the most commonly collected Cretaceous ammonites. Abundance implies ecological success, not failure.

## The Shell Parameter Connection

Normal ammonite coiling is governed by two parameters: the growth rate constant **B** (controlling tightness) and the **WER** (Whorl Expansion Ratio). In standard ammonites, B stays constant throughout growth.

In heteromorphs, B **changes** during development:

- A **Scaphites** starts with normal coiling (constant B), then B drops sharply as the shell straightens, before curving again into the hook
- A **baculite** begins with a tiny coil (high B) then the shell becomes perfectly straight (B approaches zero)
- A **gyrocone** maintains very low B throughout but with a radial offset preventing whorls from touching

## Nipponites: Is It Really Irregular?

*Nipponites mirabilis* was described when first found as the most irregular cephalopod ever discovered. It appears to be a random tangle of shell with no discernible pattern. But mathematical analysis by paleontologist Mikami (1961) and later Okamoto (1988) showed that *Nipponites* is **perfectly regular**:

The shell consists of a repeating pattern of linked U-shapes — like a chain of hairpins alternating in orientation. Each U has the same radius of curvature. The appearance of chaos arises from viewing a regular 3D pattern in two dimensions.

This has a profound implication: **the genetic instructions for building a Nipponites shell are no more complex than for any other ammonite**. The same growth program, applied differently at certain angles, produces apparent chaos. Evolution didn't need to become more complex — it just needed to change the orientation of shell secretion.

## Baculites and the Western Interior Seaway

In the Late Cretaceous (85–66 Ma), North America was split by a shallow inland sea — the **Western Interior Seaway** — running from the Gulf of Mexico to the Arctic. This sea was dominated by baculites.

Baculite mass mortality beds have been found containing thousands of individuals of the same size, suggesting **mass synchronised spawning** followed by simultaneous death — the same semelparous strategy reconstructed for coiled ammonites, but played out at enormous scale. The baculite beds of South Dakota, Montana, and Manitoba are among the most fossiliferous marine deposits on the continent.

## Lifestyle Hypotheses

Uncoiled shells raise a fundamental question: which way was up?

For **baculites**, isotopic studies of the shell chemistry suggest seasonal variation, consistent with vertical migration — the animal probably oriented vertically, drifting with its aperture facing down, catching plankton. The straight form is hydrodynamically stable in this orientation.

For **Nipponites**, the tangled shape would have oriented randomly in currents — which might actually be the point. A passive drift feeder doesn't need a preferred orientation. Its tentacles would trail in all directions, maximizing capture area.

For **Turrilites**, the helical (screwlike) shell is enigmatic. It could have been oriented with the apex up (like a snail crawling on the seafloor) or horizontally (like a free-swimmer). Finds of Turrilites in open-marine sediments suggest the latter is more likely.

## Not an Evolutionary Dead End

A persistent myth: heteromorphs are "evolutionary dead ends" that went extinct before the K-Pg event. This is false. **Scaphites** survived to within the last few hundred thousand years before the asteroid impact. **Baculites** dominated up to the end. The heteromorphs were killed not by competition or senescence, but by the same catastrophe that killed all other ammonites.

Their peculiar forms were, in the last analysis, successful adaptations to the ecological conditions of the Cretaceous — conditions that vanished in a geological instant 66 million years ago.
