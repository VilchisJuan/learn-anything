## Eight Ammonites Worth Knowing

No two ammonite species look quite alike. Shell shape, ribbing style, suture complexity, and coiling tightness vary enormously — reflecting different habitats, depths, predator pressures, and evolutionary lineages. The eight species below span 330 million years of ammonite history, from the earliest goniatites to the giant Cretaceous forms.

## Reading the Shell

Each species in the gallery tells a story through its morphology:

**Coiling tightness** (the Whorl Expansion Ratio) separates fast swimmers from drift feeders. *Goniatites* — very evolute with a large umbilicus — was likely a slow, manoeuvrable swimmer in shallow Carboniferous seas. *Placenticeras* — highly involute with a tiny umbilicus — was a faster, more streamlined Cretaceous predator.

**Ribbing and ornamentation** served multiple purposes. Coarse ribs like those of *Dactylioceras* and *Asteroceras* probably strengthened the shell against crushing-toothed predators. Ribs also acted as guides for the mantle tissue that secreted the shell, helping control chamber geometry.

**Keels** — ridges running along the outer (ventral) edge — aided streamlining. *Hildoceras* takes this furthest with its triple keel (two grooves flanking a central ridge), making it one of the most aerodynamic-looking ammonites. *Amaltheus* has a distinctive rope-like keel with a braided texture.

**Suture complexity** increased over geological time. The simple angular sutures of *Goniatites* (goniatitic pattern) gave way to the wavy ceratitic sutures of Triassic forms, and finally to the extraordinarily complex ammonitic sutures of Jurassic and Cretaceous species — providing stronger chamber walls for deeper-water lifestyles.

## Why Some Species Are Index Fossils

An **index fossil** is one that appears in rocks worldwide, evolved rapidly into distinct species, and died out quickly — making it a precise geological clock. Ammonites are the premier index fossils for the Mesozoic:

| Species | Biozone | What it dates |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| *Dactylioceras commune* | Commune Zone | Lower Toarcian (~182 Ma) |
| *Asteroceras obtusum* | Obtusum Zone | Upper Sinemurian (~196 Ma) |
| *Amaltheus margaritatus* | Margaritatus Zone | Upper Pliensbachian (~183 Ma) |
| *Hildoceras bifrons* | Bifrons Zone | Lower Toarcian (~175 Ma) |

A geologist finding *Hildoceras bifrons* in a cliff face can immediately say: "This layer is Toarcian, approximately 175 million years old." That precision — within 1–2 million years over rocks 175 million years old — is remarkable, and it's what made ammonites the backbone of Jurassic stratigraphy.

## The Giant at the End

*Parapuzosia seppenradensis* represents the culmination of gigantism in the Cretaceous. It was not rare — multiple specimens are known from Germany's Westphalia region. The animal inside a 1.8-metre shell had a body cavity large enough to contain a human. Its finely complex sutures indicate it could dive to considerable depths, using the phragmocone's buoyancy system to hover effortlessly at depth while consuming large prey.

Nothing comparable exists in the oceans today. The nautilus — ammonites' closest living relative — reaches at most 25 cm in diameter. Evolution's capacity to scale a body plan has rarely been demonstrated more dramatically than in the ammonites.
