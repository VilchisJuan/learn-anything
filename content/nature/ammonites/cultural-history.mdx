## Stones That Tell Stories

Long before paleontology existed, people found ammonite fossils and wove them into myth, medicine, and cosmology. The perfect spiral — mathematically elegant, mysteriously regular — demanded explanation. Different cultures reached for different answers, but almost all recognized that these stones were extraordinary.

## The Serpent Stones of Whitby

The cliffs of Whitby on the Yorkshire coast, England, are among the richest ammonite deposits in the world. Dactylioceras, Hildoceras, and dozens of other Jurassic species erode from the shale with every storm. Medieval residents found them in abundance — and needed an explanation.

The legend they created is one of the most enduring in English folklore: **St Hilda of Whitby** (614–680 AD), foundress of Whitby Abbey, banished a plague of serpents from the land by cutting off their heads and turning them to stone. The coiled fossils were the petrified bodies — the missing heads explained by erosion.

*Hildoceras*, the involute Jurassic ammonite with its triple keel, was named directly for St Hilda. Victorian fossil merchants sometimes carved snake heads onto ammonites to sell as "genuine serpent stones" to credulous tourists — a tradition that persisted well into the 19th century.

The Whitby coat of arms still features three snakes in reference to this legend, and the town's most famous resident, Bram Stoker, set key scenes of *Dracula* (1897) in its shadow.

## Shaligrams — Sacred Stones of Vishnu

In the Gandaki River of Nepal and the adjacent Himalayas, a specific type of ammonite fossil — **shaligrams** (or shaligrama shilas) — is considered a direct manifestation of Vishnu, the Hindu preserver god. These are typically black, smooth, ammonite-bearing nodules of limestone eroded from the ancient Tethys Sea deposits that now form the Himalayas.

Shaligrams have been sacred objects in Vaishnavite Hinduism for at least 2,000 years. The coiled interior — visible when the nodule is broken or worn — is interpreted as the **sudarshana chakra** (spinning disc weapon) of Vishnu, or as the imprint of his presence.

Pilgrims travel to the Kali Gandaki gorge — one of the deepest gorges on Earth — specifically to collect shaligrams. Different shapes and colours correspond to different avatars or aspects of Vishnu. A household with a shaligram is considered to have Vishnu himself residing within it. The stones are bathed, anointed, and worshipped daily.

This is arguably the oldest continuous tradition of ammonite veneration on Earth, practiced without interruption from ancient India to the present day.

## The Ammonis Cornu — Horns of Ammon

The scientific name "ammonite" itself traces back to ancient Egypt. The ram-headed god **Amun** (Latinised as Ammon) was among the most important deities of the Egyptian pantheon — later syncretised with Zeus by the Greeks as Zeus-Ammon. Amun was depicted wearing the curled horns of a ram.

When Roman naturalist Pliny the Elder described the coiled fossils found in North Africa, he called them **Cornu Ammonis** — "Horn of Ammon" — because their spiral recalled the god's ram's horns. The name stuck through Latin scholarly tradition: *Ammonites* was formalised as a scientific term in 1799 by zoologist Jean-Baptiste de Lamarck.

The chemical symbol for ammonia (NH₃) shares the same root — it was first derived from the dung of the animals sacrificed near the Temple of Amun at Siwa Oasis, Libya.

## Medieval European Traditions

Medieval European physicians and scholars assigned medicinal properties to ammonite fossils (then called *glossopetrae* or *snake stones*):

- **Against snake venom**: Carrying an ammonite was believed to neutralise serpent bites, in keeping with the snake-stone legend
- **Soporific**: Sleeping with an ammonite under the pillow was said to induce prophetic dreams
- **Fertility symbol**: The spiral was associated with life-energy and used in fertility rituals across Celtic and Germanic traditions

In heraldry, the coiled serpent motif — almost certainly inspired by ammonite fossils found on ancestral lands — appears in the coats of arms of several English and Scottish noble families.

## Ammolite: From Sacred Stone to Gemstone

The Blackfoot (Siksika) people of Alberta, Canada, have known ammonite fossils — particularly the iridescent ammolite specimens — for centuries. They called them **iniskim** ("buffalo calling stone") and used them in ceremonies to bring buffalo herds closer to the camp.

Ammolite — the iridescent, gem-quality surface layer of *Placenticeras* ammonites from the Bearpaw Formation — was not formally recognised as a commercial gemstone until the 1960s. By 1981, the World Jewellery Confederation had given it official gemstone status. It is now the most valuable fossil-derived gemstone, sometimes called the "7 Colours Prosperity Stone" in feng shui tradition.

Korite International, the largest ammolite mining operation, works near Lethbridge, Alberta, on deposits 75 million years old. The finest specimens — showing vivid red, green, and blue iridescence — retail for thousands of dollars per carat.

## The Mathematics of Beauty

What unites these culturally diverse traditions is a recognition that the ammonite spiral is not an ordinary shape. The **logarithmic spiral** — where distance from the centre increases exponentially — appears throughout nature: in galaxies, hurricanes, sunflower seeds, nautilus shells, and the arrangement of leaves on a stem. Mathematicians call it the *spira mirabilis* ("miraculous spiral").

The philosopher René Descartes first described it mathematically in 1638. The Swiss mathematician Jakob Bernoulli was so enchanted that he requested it carved on his tombstone — though the stonemason accidentally carved an Archimedean spiral instead.

What makes the logarithmic spiral special is **self-similarity**: zoom in or out on any section and the shape looks identical. This property — now called a fractal — means an ammonite shell looks the same at any scale. No matter how small the juvenile or how enormous the adult, the same ratio governs every coil. It is this quality — regular, recursive, infinitely scalable — that humans across cultures have found so compelling.

## Darwin and Ammonites

When Charles Darwin published *On the Origin of Species* in 1859, ammonites were among his most powerful evidence. Their 334-million-year fossil record showed continuous evolutionary change — new species arising, flourishing, going extinct, replaced by descendants. The ammonite succession through Jurassic rocks was already well documented by the 1850s, providing exactly the kind of gradual transformation Darwin's theory predicted.

Darwin wrote: "The succession of the same types of structure over all time is most striking... [ammonites] demonstrate the connection of extinct and living forms." In an era when most naturalists still believed in fixed, unchanging species, the ammonite record was uniquely difficult to dismiss.
