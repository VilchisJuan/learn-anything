## Going Further

The ammonite fossil record is among the most studied in paleontology. If you want to go deeper:

**In the field**: The Yorkshire coast (Whitby, Robin Hood's Bay), the Jurassic Coast of Dorset, and the Pierre Shale of South Dakota offer world-class ammonite collecting in accessible formations. Many specimens are findable by amateur collectors after storms expose fresh rock.

**In museums**: The Natural History Museum (London), the Senckenberg Natural History Museum (Frankfurt), the Museum of Natural History (Humboldt University, Berlin), and the Denver Museum of Nature and Science all have exceptional ammonite displays including giant *Parapuzosia* specimens.

**In research**: Ammonite paleontology is a living science. Recent advances include isotope analysis of shell chemistry to reconstruct water temperature and salinity during the animal's life, synchrotron CT scanning of septa to measure septum thickness as a depth proxy, and geometric morphometrics to quantify evolutionary shape change across lineages.

The ammonites are gone — but they left us 334 million years of stone records to read.
