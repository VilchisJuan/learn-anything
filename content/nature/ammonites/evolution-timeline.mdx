## 400 Million Years of History

Ammonoids are one of the longest-lived animal groups in the fossil record. They evolved in the Early Devonian (~400 Ma) and survived until the end of the Cretaceous (66 Ma) — a span of **334 million years**. For comparison, mammals have only existed for ~225 million years.

They survived **three of the five major mass extinctions** in Earth's history, each time rebounding with explosive diversification. They were killed only by the most catastrophic event of the last 250 million years: the Chicxulub asteroid impact.

## The Origin: Devonian Seas (419–359 Ma)

Ammonoids evolved from **bactritoids** — straight-shelled cephalopods — somewhere in the Early Devonian (~400 Ma). The earliest ammonoids, called **agoniatitids**, already showed the key feature that would define their group: a coiled shell with the siphuncle on the outer (ventral) edge.

Early Devonian seas were warm, rich, and diverse. Ammonoids occupied open-ocean niches alongside early fishes, trilobites, and the first sharks. Their goniatitic sutures — simple angular folds — were sufficient for the relatively shallow Devonian seas.

The **Late Devonian extinction** (~372–359 Ma) was actually a series of extinction pulses that eliminated ~75% of all species. Many ammonoid families were lost — but several goniatite lineages survived, and from these survivors, all later ammonoids descended.

## Carboniferous Diversification (359–299 Ma)

The Carboniferous saw ammonoids — now exclusively goniatites — diversify rapidly into the ecological space vacated by the Devonian extinctions. Hundreds of genera emerged.

The **Carboniferous seas** were warm and productive. Ammonoids ranged from tropical carbonate reefs to cooler open ocean environments. Their fossils are abundant in limestone formations that represent ancient shallow tropical seas — the same limestone that is now being quarried in places like Yorkshire (UK), Germany, and the US Midwest.

## Permian: Ceratites Arise — Then Catastrophe (299–252 Ma)

In the Permian, a new suture style evolved: **ceratitic** — with serrated lobes alongside smooth saddles. This more complex folding pattern gave ceratites stronger septa, allowing them to exploit deeper water niches.

Ammonoids were diverse and abundant in Permian seas... until **252 Ma**.

The **End-Permian extinction** — the "Great Dying" — was the worst mass extinction in Earth's history. Massive volcanic eruptions in Siberia (the Siberian Traps) flooded the atmosphere with CO₂ and SO₂, causing extreme ocean acidification, warming, and anoxia. **96% of all marine species went extinct**.

Of hundreds of ammonoid genera, only **2–3 lineages** survived into the Triassic. These survivors would give rise to all later ammonoids.

## Triassic: The Fastest Recovery Ever (252–201 Ma)

The Triassic ammonoid recovery is one of the most spectacular evolutionary radiations in the fossil record. From just a handful of surviving lineages at 252 Ma, ammonoids had diversified into **hundreds of genera within 5–10 million years** — faster than almost any comparable recovery event.

The key innovation in the Triassic: **ammonitic sutures**. Starting from ceratitic ancestors, some lineages evolved the highly complex, multi-folded ammonitic suture pattern — dramatically stronger septa that enabled them to live at much greater depths.

These animals, the true **ammonites** (Order Ammonitida), would dominate for the next 185 million years.

Another mass extinction struck at the end of the Triassic (~201 Ma), linked to massive volcanism as Pangaea broke apart (the Central Atlantic Magmatic Province, CAMP). Again, ammonites crashed — and again, survivors radiated explosively in the Early Jurassic.

## Jurassic: The Golden Age (201–145 Ma)

The Jurassic was the golden age of ammonites. They achieved their **greatest global diversity**, with tens of thousands of species inhabiting every marine environment from tropical lagoons to polar seas.

Jurassic ammonites were extraordinarily diverse in form:
- Tightly coiled involute forms like *Quenstedtoceras*
- Widely coiled evolute forms like *Perisphinctes*
- Ribbed, tubercled, keeled, smooth — every surface texture existed
- Sizes from a centimetre to over a metre

**They were the dominant large invertebrates of Jurassic seas.** So common were their fossils that many Jurassic rock formations are subdivided into "biozones" — time intervals of as little as 200,000 years — defined by the appearance of specific ammonite species.

## Cretaceous: Heteromorphs and Final Extinction (145–66 Ma)

The Cretaceous brought a new evolutionary experiment: **heteromorph ammonites** — species that partially or completely uncoiled their shells. Hook-shaped, turreted, helical, paper-clip, and worm-like forms appeared and diversified.

These were not accidents or diseased specimens. They were **successful ecological specialists**, probably adapted for slow drift-feeding in the water column (using their bizarre shapes as stabilizers) or for benthic (seafloor) life.

Some Cretaceous ammonites achieved enormous sizes. *Parapuzosia seppenradensis*, from the Late Cretaceous of Germany, had a shell diameter of **1.8 metres** — the largest ammonite ever found. *Parapuzosia* from the US Western Interior Seaway reached 2 metres.

Then, at precisely **66.043 Ma**, everything changed.

The [Chicxulub asteroid](/nature/ammonites/extinction) — a 10-km bolide — struck the Yucatán Peninsula. The resulting global catastrophe eliminated all ammonites within years to decades. Not a single species survived.

## Why Did They Survive Three Extinctions But Not the Fourth?

This is one of paleontology's most fascinating questions. Three times, ammonoids were nearly wiped out and recovered. But the K-Pg event was different:

1. **Impact speed**: The K-Pg event was sudden — no gradual warm-up. Previous extinctions gave millions of years for incremental adaptation.

2. **Reproductive vulnerability**: Ammonites had tiny, planktonic larvae that required surface-ocean productivity. The impact winter shut down surface productivity almost instantly. Larvae starved before maturing.

3. **Ocean acidification**: The impact caused rapid ocean acidification from both the impact itself and ensuing volcanic CO₂ — possibly dissolving calcium carbonate shells rapidly.

4. **Nautiloids survived**: Their strategy of large, yolk-rich deep-sea eggs — independent of surface plankton — carried them through. Ammonites' strategy failed.

The fossil record tells us: ammonoids were extraordinarily resilient. The K-Pg event was simply beyond any adaptation possible in the available time.
