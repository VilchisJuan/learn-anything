## How Did Ammonites Live?

Ammonites are extinct, so we cannot observe them directly. But we can infer a great deal from three sources:

1. **The fossil record** — abundance, distribution, shell morphology, stomach contents, bite marks
2. **Living analogues** — nautiluses, squids, and octopuses as behavioural models
3. **Physical modelling** — hydrodynamic studies of shell shapes in tanks and computer simulations

The picture that emerges is of **highly diverse ecological strategies** — ammonites were not all doing the same thing. Different shell shapes almost certainly corresponded to different lifestyles.

## Locomotion: Jet-Propelled Swimmers

Like all cephalopods, ammonites moved by **jet propulsion**: drawing water into a muscular mantle cavity and expelling it forcefully through a flexible siphon tube (the hyponome), which could be aimed to steer direction.

However, the heavy coiled shell limited their speed compared to modern squids. Most ammonites were probably:

- **Slow, manoeuvrable swimmers** hovering at specific depths using buoyancy control
- **Drifting or near-passive** in some forms — especially the bizarre uncoiled heteromorphs
- **Capable of rapid escape bursts** when threatened, like modern nautiluses

The buoyancy system (gas in the [phragmocone](/nature/ammonites/anatomy), controlled by the siphuncle) was crucial. By pumping gas into or out of chambers, ammonites could adjust their neutral buoyancy depth — floating effortlessly at whatever depth they chose, expending energy only on horizontal movement.

Different ammonite species lived at very different depths. Shell morphology and suture complexity are our best proxies: more complex sutures mean stronger septa that could resist greater water pressure.

## What Did They Eat?

Direct evidence is rare, but several lines of evidence suggest ammonites were **opportunistic predators and scavengers**:

- **Preserved stomach contents** in exceptionally preserved specimens include crustacean fragments, small fish, worms, and plankton
- **Jaw and radula fossils** — ammonites had a beak-like jaw (aptychi) and a toothed rasping tongue (radula), indicating they could bite and rasp prey
- **Shell morphology** — large, smooth, fast-moving ammonites were probably active predators; small, heavily ornamented forms may have fed on bottom organisms or plankton

Some species — especially the slow-moving hook-shaped heteromorphs like *Nipponites* — were probably **drift feeders**: slowly drifting with tentacles spread, catching zooplankton from the water column.

## A Staggering Size Range

Ammonites occupied an enormous size range across species and across life stages:

| Species | Diameter | Environment |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| *Vermiceras* (juvenile) | 3–5 mm | Near-shore |
| *Dactylioceras* (typical adult) | 5–10 cm | Shallow shelf |
| *Titanites* | up to 53 cm | Open water |
| *Parapuzosia seppenradensis* | **1.8 metres** | Open ocean |

A 2-metre ammonite eating fish and large invertebrates likely lived for decades. Its phragmocone would have withstood enormous pressure at depth.

## Sexual Dimorphism: Two Sizes, One Species

Many ammonite species occur in two distinct size classes within the same rock layer:

- **Macroconchs** — larger individuals, up to 3× bigger
- **Microconchs** — smaller, often with modified apertures (lappets, hooks)

The consensus is that these are **females (macroconch)** and **males (microconch)** of the same species. The female needed greater body mass to produce eggs; the male's modified aperture may have served in mating displays.

Remarkably, dozens of "different species" described in the 1800s have since been reunited as macroconch-microconch pairs. The famous *Quenstedtoceras* / *Kepplerites* "pair" — treated as separate genera for over a century — are likely male and female of one species.

## Predators

Ammonites were prey as well as predators. The fossil record preserves evidence of attacks:

- **Mosasaur bite marks** — curved tooth impressions matching known mosasaur dentition, found on Late Cretaceous ammonites. Some show healed damage, meaning the ammonite survived at least one attack
- **Drill holes** — predatory gastropods (snails) drilled through the shells of small ammonites
- **Crushed shells** — suggesting predation by crushing-toothed predators

Some mosasaur species evolved rounded crushing teeth — possibly specialised for cracking ammonite shells. The apex marine predators of the Cretaceous and the most abundant large invertebrates were locked in an evolutionary arms race.

## Did Ammonites School?

One intriguing hypothesis: some ammonites may have **schooled like fish**. Evidence:

- Deposits containing **thousands of same-size ammonites** together suggest mass aggregation
- A single size class implies **mass spawning** followed by simultaneous death (like modern squids after spawning)
- Shell hydrodynamics suggest passive alignment in currents — a shoal might naturally stay together

We cannot prove schooling from fossils alone. But mass mortality deposits are consistent with aggregation — for reproduction, migration through currents, or passive concentration after death.

## Life Cycle

Ammonites hatched from small eggs (protoconch diameter ~0.5–1 mm). Growth was probably rapid in juveniles, reaching adult size within 1–5 years depending on species.

The presence of distinct macroconch/microconch forms suggests adult size was reached after a set number of growth stages, after which the aperture was sealed or modified. After sexual maturity, the animal may have spawned once and died — a **semelparous** strategy, like Pacific salmon.

This strategy — explosive growth, early reproduction, mass death — helps explain why ammonite fossils are so extraordinarily abundant. In favourable environments, ammonite populations would have grown explosively, filling the water column.
